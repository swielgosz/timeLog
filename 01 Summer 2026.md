**CR3BP metric(s):**
Our neural ODE is learning to predict the CR3BP acceleration vector at any point in p-hase space. Given a state $[x, y, z, v_x, v_y, v_z]$ in the rotating frame, it ouputs $[a_x, a_y, a_z]$. The model is trying to approximate the true acceleration. Our precious metric was mean acceleration error averaged over all trajectory points in the test dataset (e.g. we take our test orbits and at each timestep for each orbit, we calculate the absolute acceleration percent error, then average over all of these points). This isn't the best metric because it isn't weighted by scientific importance - we might care a lot how the model performs near L1 and L2, but we don't care as much near L3 since we don't have missions there. 

Idea for a metric - rather than sampling along test trajectories, place a fixed grid of points across the entire cislunar phase space and evaluate the acceleration error at each one. Then, weight each error by how much we care about each location.
$E(x) = w_{\text{region}}(x) * w_\text{manifold}(x) * ||a_\text{true}(x) - a_\text{model}(x)||$
Terms:
- $w_{\text{region}}(x)$  - this tells us how scientifically important the region we are in is.
$$
w_{\mathrm{region}}(\mathbf{x})
=
\sum_{i=1}^{5}
\alpha_i
\exp\!\left(
-\frac{d(\mathbf{x},L_i)}{\sigma}
\right),

$$

	where $\alpha_i$ is the weighting factor for each Lagrange points (this sets the scientific value), and $\sigma$ defines a radius of influence. We use this because the lagrange points have differently sized dynamical neighborhoods. Example - L1/L2 neighborhoods are much smaller (~.15 canonical units) than L4/L5.
The final scalar is the weighted mean of $E(x)$ across all grid points. By formulating the error this way, we are able to both visualize where in phase space the error is the highest, as well as report a single scalar.

Yes, but with important differences:

**L1 and L2** — unstable equilibria (index-1 saddles), so they have stable and unstable manifolds in the classical sense. The periodic orbits around them (halos, Lyapunov) also have their own manifolds, and these are the ones used for mission design. This is the well-studied case.

Right, so the real question is: how do you measure acceleration error in a way that's **spatially meaningful** rather than just averaging over whatever trajectories happen to be in your training set?

The answer is to decouple the error measurement from the training trajectories entirely. Instead of evaluating acceleration error along integrated trajectories (which are biased toward wherever your training data lives), you:

1. **Sample ICs on a structured grid** across the cislunar phase space — organized by position and Jacobi constant level, weighted toward L1/L2
2. **Evaluate the acceleration error pointwise at those grid locations** — no integration needed, just query the true CR3BP acceleration and the model acceleration at each point
3. **Plot as a spatial heatmap** — you see exactly where in phase space the model is getting the dynamics wrong

This preserves acceleration error as your core quantity (which is the right thing to measure since that's what the model is learning) but gives it the phase space coverage and spatial interpretability that the naive trajectory-averaged version lacks.

The Jacobi constant drift idea is still useful but as a secondary check — it tells you whether the accumulated error along trajectories is physically harmful, whereas the grid acceleration error tells you the root cause and where it's coming from.

So to summarize the two-tier system more precisely:

- **Primary**: grid-sampled acceleration error heatmap — diagnoses _where_ the model is wrong
- **Secondary**: Jacobi drift along integrated trajectories — diagnoses _how badly_ those errors compound

  
is there a way we can marry the ideas of measuring dynamics fidelity, spatial awareness of how close we are to lagrange points (and maybe manifolds)?

Yes — and I think the cleanest way to do it is a **weighted acceleration error on a structured grid**, where the weights encode both spatial importance and dynamical sensitivity.

Concretely, at each grid point you compute:

```
weighted_error(x) = w_region(x) * w_manifold(x) * ||a_true(x) - a_model(x)||
```

Where:

**`w_region(x)`** — distance-based weight that increases as you get closer to L1/L2, reflects the scientific importance hierarchy you described. Something like `1 / (1 + d(x, L_i))` for the nearest relevant libration point.

**`w_manifold(x)`** — measures how close the point is to a known manifold surface. Points near the manifold tubes get higher weight because errors there directly corrupt the mission-design-relevant dynamics. This naturally incorporates manifold fidelity without the exponential sensitivity problem — you're not integrating along manifolds, just weighting grid points by proximity to them.

This gives you a single scalar metric when aggregated, but it also produces a **spatial heatmap** when you keep it disaggregated — so you can see exactly where the model is failing and why.

The nice properties:

- Measures acceleration error directly — stays true to what the model is learning
- Spatially aware via `w_region`
- Manifold-aware via `w_manifold` without the instability problem
- Works everywhere in phase space, not just near periodic orbits
- Naturally extensible — you can add more weight terms later (e.g. weighting by Jacobi constant level)

The exact values are tunable, but the ratios encode your scientific priority. A few design choices worth thinking about:

**Distance in what space?** Just position (x,y,z) is simplest, but the libration points are equilibria in the full 6D phase space. **On intelligent gridding:**

The key insight is that you don't need to evaluate on a dense uniform grid — you need **coverage of the important regions** without wasting evaluations in unimportant ones. A few strategies:

**Stratified sampling by Jacobi constant** Rather than a uniform 6D grid (which is intractable), fix a set of C values spanning the cislunar energy range and sample position/velocity pairs consistent with each C level. This keeps you on physically meaningful surfaces and naturally constrains the sampling.

**Importance-weighted sampling density** Sample more points near L1/L2 and fewer near L3/far cislunar — directly matching the `w_region` weighting. Points that will have low weight anyway don't need dense coverage.

**Positional grid only, velocity from C** Fix a 2D or 3D positional grid in the xy-plane (or xz for 3D), then for each position sample a small number of velocities consistent with a target C value. This reduces a 6D problem to a manageable 2D/3D one.

**Precompute and cache the grid** The grid points and their true accelerations are fixed — they don't depend on the model. Precompute `a_true` and all weights once, save to disk, and at evaluation time you only need to query the model at those fixed points. Model evaluation at N points is a single batched forward pass, so even N=10,000 should be fast.

The caching point is probably the most important — evaluation cost becomes just one forward pass through the model regardless of grid size.

That's a valid concern — the Jacobi constant varies very little across a halo family (e.g. the L1 halo family spans roughly C ∈ [3.0, 3.2] in EM canonical units), so discretizing by C value risks either redundant coverage or missing important regions entirely.

A better organizing principle for the grid might be **position-space coverage** rather than C-space coverage. Concretely:

- Place a positional grid directly in the cislunar region (x, y for planar or x, z for 3D)
- At each grid point, sample a **few velocities from a range of C values** rather than one specific C
- This way the grid covers space naturally, and the velocity sampling gives you some spread in energy without needing to carefully choose C levels

The C values you do sample at each position could be chosen relative to the local critical values — e.g. sample at `C_L1 + δ`, `C_L1`, `C_L1 - δ` for points near L1. This keeps the sampling physically meaningful without requiring precise C discrimination across orbit families.

This also sidesteps the deeper issue: the Jacobi constant is more useful as a **normalization tool** (for `w_region`) than as a **sampling axis**. The thing that actually varies meaningfully across the phase space is position — that's what determines which dynamical regime you're in. C is a consequence of where you are and how fast you're moving, not an independent axis worth gridding over.

What is an invariant manifold? For a dynamical system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ with a flow map $\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)$, an invariant manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is a set of states that maps into itself under the dynamics $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathcal{M} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) \in \mathcal{M}$. In CR3BP trajectory design, these manifolds are usually associated with an equilibrium point or a periodic orbit like Lyapunov, halo, NRHO orbits. **In plain terms, an invariant manifold is a geometric set in phase space that the dynamics cannot leave.**

The manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is invariant if starting on it means the dynamics keeps you on it: $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathcal{M} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) \in \mathcal{M}.$

In words, if you start on the manifold you will stay there.

What is a flow map? A flow map is the oeprator that takes an initial state and returns the state reached after evolving under the dynamics for some time - basically it is representing propagation of an initial condition under specified dynamics. In words: $\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) = \text{the point reached by propagating } \mathbf{x}_0 \text{ forward by time } t$, e.g. it is the exact nonlinear propagation operator for the dynamical system.

The STM is related but not the same object. The flow map is nonlinear in general $\mathbf{x}(t) = \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)$, while the STM is the local linearization of that flow map with respect to the initial condition $\mathbf{\Phi}(t,0) = \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}(t)}{\partial \mathbf{x}_0} = \frac{\partial \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)}{\partial \mathbf{x}_0}$. So, the STM is the Jacobian of the flow map with repsect to the initial state. The flow map tells you where a particular initial condition goes, but a STM tells you how a small perturbation to that initial condition evolves. 

> [!note]- Gradient vs Jacobian
> The gradient is the vector formed by the partial derivatives of a _scalar_ function.
> 
> The Jacobian matrix is the matrix formed by the partial derivatives of a _vector_ function. Its vectors are the gradients of the respective components of the function.
> 
> E.g., with some argument omissions,
> 
> ∇𝑓(𝑥,𝑦)=(𝑓′𝑥𝑓′𝑦)
> 
> 𝐽(𝑓(𝑥,𝑦),𝑔(𝑥,𝑦))=(𝑓′𝑥𝑓′𝑦𝑔′𝑥𝑔′𝑦)=(∇𝑓;∇𝑔).
> 
> If you want, the Jacobian is a generalization of the gradient to vector functions

> [!note]- Monodromy matrix as it relates to stable and unstable manifolds
> For a periodic orbit, the stable and unstable manifolds are generated from the linearized dynamics around the orbit. You compute the monodromy matrix, which is the state transition matrix over one full period:
> 
> \mathbf{\Phi}(T,0).
> 
> Its eigenvalues and eigenvectors describe the local behavior near the periodic orbit. If the periodic orbit has one real eigenvalue outside the unit circle,
> 
> |\lambda_u| > 1,
> 
> then the corresponding eigenvector gives the local unstable direction. Perturbing the periodic orbit slightly along that direction and propagating forward produces the unstable manifold.
> 
> If it has one real eigenvalue inside the unit circle,
> 
> |\lambda_s| < 1,
> 
> then the corresponding eigenvector gives the local stable direction. Perturbing along that direction and propagating backward is a common way to trace out the stable manifold. Forward in time, those trajectories approach the periodic orbit.
> 
> A quasi-stable manifold is a less universal term. It is not as mathematically standard as stable or unstable manifold. In astrodynamics, people may use “quasi-stable” to describe trajectories or structures that remain near a region for a long time but are not truly asymptotically stable.
> 
> That distinction matters. A true stable manifold has an asymptotic property:
> 
> \operatorname{dist}\left(\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0), \gamma\right)
> \to 0
> \quad \text{as} \quad t \to +\infty.
> 
> A quasi-stable trajectory may only satisfy something weaker, such as:
> 
> \operatorname{dist}\left(\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0), \gamma\right)
> \text{ remains small for a long but finite time.}
> 
> So “quasi-stable” usually means “temporarily stable-looking” or “weakly escaping,” not truly stable in the invariant-manifold sense.
> Stable and unstable manifolds are both kinds of invariant manifolds.

A **Hamiltonian system** describes dynamics in terms of an energy-like scalar function called the **Hamiltonian**:

$$
H(q,p)
$$

where $q$ represents generalized position coordinates and $p$ represents generalized momentum coordinates.

For many mechanical systems, the Hamiltonian is the total mechanical energy:

$$
H(q,p) = T(p) + V(q),
$$

so

$$
\text{Hamiltonian} = \text{kinetic energy} + \text{potential energy}.
$$

Instead of saying “forces cause accelerations,” Hamiltonian mechanics says that the **energy landscape determines how position and momentum evolve through phase space**.

The equations of motion are Hamilton’s equations:

$$
\dot{q} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p},
\qquad
\dot{p} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}.
$$

The first equation says that the way energy changes with momentum determines how position evolves. The second says that the way energy changes with position determines how momentum evolves, with a minus sign. That minus sign is what gives Hamiltonian motion its characteristic circulating behavior in phase space.

For a simple mass-spring system,

$$
H(q,p)
=
\frac{p^2}{2m}
+
\frac{1}{2}kq^2.
$$

The system trades energy between kinetic and potential energy. When the mass is far from equilibrium, energy is mostly potential. When it passes through equilibrium, energy is mostly kinetic. In phase space $(q,p)$, the trajectory forms closed loops because total energy is conserved.

So a useful intuition is that Hamiltonian motion follows contours of constant total energy in phase space

It does not usually move “downhill” like a dissipative system. Instead, it moves **around** the energy landscape. That is why Hamiltonian systems are strongly associated with conservative dynamics, oscillators, orbital motion, and phase-space structure.

For example, in orbital mechanics, the two-body problem can be written as a Hamiltonian system:

$$
H(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{p})
=
\frac{\|\mathbf{p}\|^2}{2m}
-
\frac{\mu m}{\|\mathbf{r}\|}.
$$

The first term is kinetic energy and the second term is gravitational potential energy. In the ideal two-body problem, the spacecraft does not spiral inward or outward because no energy is being added or removed. Its trajectory is constrained by the constant-energy structure of the system.

A useful contrast is a gradient system. A gradient system moves downhill on a scalar function:

$$
\dot{x} = -\nabla V(x).
$$

A Hamiltonian system instead moves perpendicular, in a structured sense, to the gradient of the Hamiltonian:

$$
\dot{x} = J \nabla H(x),
$$

where

$$
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
q \\
p
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
J =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & I \\
-I & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The matrix $J$ rotates the energy gradient into the direction of motion. That is why the system circulates around energy contours instead of descending them.

Intuitively, a Hamiltonian system is a conservative dynamical system where the total-energy function defines the geometry of motion. The state evolves through phase space in a way that preserves energy and follows structured position-momentum coupling.

**Hamiltonian in the CR3BP:**

In the circular restricted three-body problem, the Hamiltonian depends on the coordinate frame. The most common CR3BP formulation uses the **synodic rotating frame**, where the two primaries are fixed on the $x$-axis. In nondimensional units, the Hamiltonian is usually written as

$$
H(x,y,z,p_x,p_y,p_z)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2\right)
+
y p_x
-
x p_y
-
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
-
\frac{\mu}{r_2}.
$$

Here,

$$
r_1 = \sqrt{(x+\mu)^2+y^2+z^2},
$$

$$
r_2 = \sqrt{(x-1+\mu)^2+y^2+z^2},
$$

where $\mu$ is the nondimensional mass parameter,

$$
\mu = \frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}.
$$

The larger primary is located at

$$
(-\mu,0,0),
$$

and the smaller primary is located at

$$
(1-\mu,0,0).
$$

The canonical momenta are not simply the velocities in the rotating frame. They are

$$
p_x = \dot{x} - y,
$$

$$
p_y = \dot{y} + x,
$$

$$
p_z = \dot{z}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\dot{x} = p_x + y,
$$

$$
\dot{y} = p_y - x,
$$

$$
\dot{z} = p_z.
$$

The Hamiltonian can also be written in terms of the rotating-frame velocities as

$$
H
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2+\dot{z}^2\right)
-
\frac{1}{2}(x^2+y^2)
-
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
-
\frac{\mu}{r_2}.
$$

This form makes the connection to the Jacobi constant clearer. Define the pseudo-potential

$$
\Omega(x,y,z)
=
\frac{1}{2}(x^2+y^2)
+
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
+
\frac{\mu}{r_2}.
$$

Then the CR3BP equations of motion are often written as

$$
\ddot{x} - 2\dot{y} = \frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial x},
$$

$$
\ddot{y} + 2\dot{x} = \frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial y},
$$

$$
\ddot{z} = \frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial z}.
$$

The Jacobi constant is

$$
C
=
2\Omega
-
\left(\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2+\dot{z}^2\right).
$$

Using the velocity-form Hamiltonian above,

$$
H
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2+\dot{z}^2\right)
-
\Omega,
$$

so

$$
C = -2H.
$$

Intuitively, the CR3BP Hamiltonian is the conserved energy-like quantity in the rotating frame. It combines kinetic energy, gravitational potential from both primaries, and the apparent potential associated with the rotating frame. The extra canonical terms

$$
y p_x - x p_y
$$

appear because the rotating frame introduces Coriolis-type coupling between position and momentum.

So the compact answer is:

$$
\boxed{
H
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2\right)
+
y p_x
-
x p_y
-
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
-
\frac{\mu}{r_2}
}
$$

with

$$
\boxed{
C=-2H.
}
$$

What is a Hessian? It is the square matrix of all the second-order partial derivatives of a multivariable function. For a scalar function $f(\mathbf{x})$, where $\mathbf{x} = [x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n]^T$, the Hessian is 
$$
\nabla^2 f(\mathbf{x})
=
\mathbf{H}_f(\mathbf{x})
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} &
\cdots &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_n} \\

\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_1} &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} &
\cdots &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_n} \\

\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\

\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_1} &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_2} &
\cdots &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2}
\end{bmatrix}
$$
