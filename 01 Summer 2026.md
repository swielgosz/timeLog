**On the family of orbits:**

This is the most important one to push back on. Training on 4 Lyapunov orbits at different amplitudes samples 4 curves in 4D phase space — together they cover a 2D surface (the orbit family manifold). The stable and unstable manifolds of each orbit are transverse to this family manifold. They live in the other 2 directions of the 4D space that the training data doesn't touch.

You can check this explicitly: compute the angle between the family continuation direction (the vector from one orbit's IC to the next orbit's IC in phase space) and the unstable eigenvector at the same point. If they're nearly orthogonal, the family gives essentially zero coverage in the unstable direction. If they overlap significantly, the family does some of the work. This is an empirical question worth computing before assuming the family is sufficient.

**On segmentation removing the IC notion:**

You're right, and this is actually more of a problem than it first appears. Segmentation turns the training into: given this state on the orbit, predict the next 20 steps. The model sees many states along the orbit as independent starting points. But — and this is the key — all of those starting states are on the orbit. In 4D phase space the model is still only seeing states on the 1D orbit curve. It has complete freedom to define whatever dynamics it wants in the 2 directions transverse to the orbit, and it picks the path of least resistance: smooth, neutral dynamics.

**On CR3BP sensitivity making off-orbit training hard:**

The sensitivity is only dangerous over long integration times. Over a very short segment — say 0.01T — a perturbation of ε in the unstable direction grows by 2600^0.01 ≈ 1.08. Barely anything. You could train on extremely short trajectories from perturbed ICs and the training signal would be useful without the trajectory escaping. The sensitivity is not a fundamental barrier to off-orbit training; it just requires short enough segments that the instability hasn't had time to act.

**But here's the deeper adversarial point that cuts across all three:**

This is a non-identifiability problem. With data only on the orbit curves, there are infinitely many vector fields consistent with the training data — any smooth function that agrees on those curves works. The model is finding one of those infinitely many solutions. It happens to be the wrong one because the optimizer prefers smooth, neutral dynamics. No amount of better optimization, more orbits in the family, or cleverer segmentation can fix non-identifiability. You need either more informative data (off-orbit trajectories) or additional constraints that rule out the wrong solutions.

The constraint you're willing to use: Jacobi conservation. This is physically meaningful, doesn't require knowing the acceleration directly, and rules out a large class of wrong solutions — specifically any dynamics that don't conserve energy. A model that conserves Jacobi can't have λ_u_model ≈ 1 for an orbit with λ_u_true ≈ 2600, because a Jacobi-conserving model with neutral stability would produce a family of quasi-periodic orbits, which is a qualitatively different phase space structure from the true saddle. Adding Jacobi drift as an auxiliary loss is the most principled thing you can do within your stated constraints.

**What to actually do next:**

Before any of that, run the single-orbit overfit test. Train on exactly 1 orbit with no curriculum, maximum steps, and log gradient norms explicitly. This tells you whether the issue is capacity, optimization, or data. If the model can't overfit 1 orbit with direct access to all gradient information, the problem is architectural and everything else is irrelevant.

What are floquet multipliers? What is a useful sort of perturbation analysis that we could do 

The monodromy matrix depends on which point on the orbit you choose as your starting point. If you start at point A and integrate for one full period back to A, you get M_A. If you start at point B (somewhere else on the same orbit) and integrate for one full period back to B, you get a different matrix M_B.

**But the eigenvalues are the same regardless of where you start.**

This is provable algebraically. If you start at B instead of A, the monodromy matrix transforms as:

```
M_B = Φ(B→A) · M_A · Φ(A→B)
```

where Φ(A→B) is the flow map from A to B. This is called a **similarity transformation**, and similarity transformations preserve eigenvalues. So λ_u ≈ 2600 is a property of the orbit itself, not of any particular starting point on it.

**The eigenvectors do change with the evaluation point.** The unstable direction at point A in phase space points in a different direction than the unstable direction at point B — the manifold curves as you move along the orbit. What stays constant is the growth rate, not the direction.

## What this means for our computation

We computed M at the training IC — the first point in the orbit states, which for Lyapunov orbits is typically the x-axis crossing. That's a natural and convenient choice.

The λ_u ≈ 2600 we got is a global property of each orbit and is correct regardless of where we evaluated it. The eigenvectors we extracted are the stable/unstable directions _at that specific point_, which is why we used them to perturb the IC and not some arbitrary other point on the orbit.

If we wanted to probe the model's accuracy in the stable/unstable directions at other points along the orbit, we'd need to propagate the eigenvectors forward using the state transition matrix — but for diagnosing the model we don't need that level of detail yet.

**Adversarial point on "instability blows up gradients":**

When you say instability blows up gradients, you're describing a symptom, not the cause. The actual mechanism is this: the gradient of the loss flows backward through the ODE solver via the adjoint equations. That backward propagation involves the state transition matrix Φ(T, 0) — which is the monodromy matrix. If λ_u ≈ 2600, then the condition number of Φ(T, 0) is roughly λ_u / λ_s ≈ 2600 / 0.0004 ≈ 6.5 million. A condition number that large means the gradient computation is numerically ill-conditioned — small errors in the loss get amplified enormously when you backpropagate through a full period.

So segmentation isn't just a hack to keep gradients small. It's a direct response to the instability that the Floquet multipliers describe. Shorter segments mean smaller Φ(τ, 0), smaller condition number, more stable gradient flow.

**But here's the adversarial version of that connection:**

Segmentation solves the gradient problem by preventing the optimizer from ever seeing the consequences of the instability. Over a segment of length 0.3T, the unstable growth is λ_u^0.3 ≈ 20x instead of 2600x. The loss gradient is tractable — but it's also informationally blind to the full instability. The model never receives a training signal that says "your predicted dynamics in the unstable direction is wrong by a factor of 2600." It only ever sees "your trajectory is off by a manageable amount after 0.3 periods."

So the same property that makes training hard (ill-conditioned gradients over full periods) also creates the failure mode (model doesn't learn the stability structure). You cannot fix both with the same workaround. Segmentation trades one problem for another.

---

**On computing monodromy at multiple orbit points:**

Here's where I want to push back on your intuition. You might be thinking "more evaluation points gives more information." But recall what we established: for the **true** dynamics, the Floquet multipliers are invariant around the orbit — λ_u ≈ 2600 everywhere. So computing M_true at 4 different phases gives you 4 identical sets of eigenvalues. Zero additional information about the orbit's stability.

**However** — and this is where it gets interesting — that invariance only holds for the true dynamics because those dynamics are actually consistent and the orbit is actually periodic. The model is not guaranteed to have either property. The model might produce a trajectory that _approximately_ closes after one period (hence low percent error at t=T) without having a true periodic orbit or internally consistent dynamics.

So for **M_model**, the eigenvalues are NOT constrained to be the same at different phases. If you compute M_model at four points around the orbit and get four different λ_u_model values, that's telling you the model doesn't even have a coherent notion of what the dynamics look like around the orbit. That's a different — and deeper — failure mode than consistently having λ_u_model ≈ 1.

Concretely, you might find:

- **λ_u_model ≈ 1 at all phases**: the model consistently thinks the orbit is stable — a uniform, systematic wrong belief
- **λ_u_model wildly varying (e.g., 0.5 to 50)**: the model has internally inconsistent dynamics — it's stitching together locally different vector fields that happen to produce an approximately closed trajectory when started from the training IC
- **λ_u_model increasing toward the correct value at the training IC phase and degrading elsewhere**: phase-anchoring in the stability structure, not just phase-anchoring in trajectory error

That third pattern would be the most revealing. It would confirm that the model learned _something_ about the instability near the training IC but failed to generalize it around the orbit — which is a much more specific diagnosis than just "wrong Floquet multipliers."

So yes, worth implementing — but the reason it's useful is not "more data points about the orbit's stability." It's "a consistency check on whether the model has coherent dynamics at all."






**The adjoint method and what decays:**

In the adjoint formulation, the gradient integrand is `a(t)^T ∂f/∂θ`. The adjoint state `a(t)` starts at `t1` (initialized from the loss gradient) and propagates _backward_ in time. The adjoint ODE is:

```
da/dt = -a(t)^T ∂f/∂z
```

The key is: the adjoint propagates backward through the same Jacobian structure that governs the forward instability. And here's the precise Floquet connection — the adjoint propagator over one period backward has eigenvalues that are the **reciprocals** of the Floquet multipliers.

So for our orbit:

- Forward unstable direction: λ_u ≈ 2600 — trajectories diverge
- Adjoint in that direction (going backward): 1/2600 ≈ 0.0004 — **the adjoint decays**

This means the gradient is vanishing specifically in the unstable direction. The signal about "your parameters controlling the unstable mode are wrong" decays by a factor of 0.0004 per period of backward propagation. By the time you reach early timesteps, that signal is effectively zero.

**But there's a second simultaneous problem you didn't mention:**

The stable direction has the reciprocal behavior. λ_s ≈ 0.0004 forward, so the adjoint grows by 1/0.0004 ≈ 2500 going backward in the stable direction. That's a potentially _exploding_ gradient. So you have:

- Vanishing gradient in the direction that matters most (unstable)
- Potentially exploding gradient in the other direction (stable)

Both come from the same large condition number of the backward propagator — roughly 2600/0.0004 ≈ 6.5 million. Segmentation reduces the condition number to λ_u^0.3 / λ_s^0.3 — still large but tractable.

**Now the adversarial point — and this is important:**

Your segmentation explanation assumes the problem is purely numerical: the gradients vanish, segmentation fixes that, and then the model can learn correctly. But the Floquet analysis reveals something more fundamental.

The gradient vanishes _specifically in the unstable direction_. That means even if you could somehow stabilize the adjoint computation over a full period, the gradient signal telling the model "your dynamics in the unstable direction are wrong" would still be small — because the adjoint is inherently suppressed in exactly that direction by the forward instability structure.

Think about what this means: the model receives essentially no gradient signal about its behavior in the unstable manifold direction, regardless of segment length. Segmentation helps stabilize the computation, but it doesn't create a gradient signal that was never there. The loss function — position error along the nominal trajectory — is simply not sensitive to errors in the unstable direction over any timescale short enough to have tractable gradients.

**So to directly answer your question:** Yes, there is a tight Floquet connection. The vanishing gradient and the trajectory instability are the same phenomenon, viewed from two sides. The number λ_u ≈ 2600 simultaneously causes trajectories to escape in one period _and_ causes the adjoint to lose sensitivity to early parameter errors in one period. Segmentation is a patch on the computational problem, not on the fundamental one: the training objective has no useful gradient signal about stability structure at any tractable training horizon.

**On computing monodromy at multiple orbit points:**

This is worth doing, but push back on why. The true Floquet multipliers are invariant — you'll get λ_u ≈ 2600 everywhere — so you learn nothing new about the orbit. But the model's eigenvalues are not constrained to be invariant. If you compute M_model at phases 0, T/4, T/2, 3T/4 and get wildly different λ_u_model values, that tells you the model doesn't have internally consistent dynamics — it's stitching together locally different vector fields that happen to produce an approximately closed trajectory from the training IC. That's a deeper failure than uniformly wrong multipliers, and it's a failure the other diagnostics can't see. It would also tell you something about whether the model's errors are localized to certain phases or uniform around the orbit — which has implications for what to fix.

You don't need specific family Jacobi constants for a crude first pass — the critical values C_Li are already a natural anchor. Each orbit family bifurcates from its libration point at exactly C = C_Li and grows toward lower C as orbits get larger. So C_Li is the characteristic energy of "small orbits near Li," and the weighting naturally de-emphasizes larger orbits (which is arguably correct — you're most interested in the dynamically active region near the point itself).

The formula would be:

```
w_jacobi(state) = Σᵢ λᵢ · exp(-(C(state) - C_Li)² / (2σ_C²))
```

where C(state) = 2Ω(x,y) - v² is the Jacobi constant at that grid state, and the λᵢ encode your importance ranking:

```
λ_L1 = λ_L2 = 1.0,  λ_L4 = λ_L5 = 0.5,  λ_L3 = 0.1
```

This directly captures what you want:

- A state far from L1 in position but with C ≈ C_L1 (the far end of a large Lyapunov orbit) gets high weight
- A state near L1 but with C far from C_L1 (energetically incompatible with L1 dynamics) gets low weight
- L3 is de-emphasized regardless of position

The crude part is σ_C. A reasonable starting point is σ_C ≈ 0.05, since |C_L1 - C_L2| ≈ 0.016 and |C_L2 - C_L3| ≈ 0.16 — tight enough to separate L1 from L2 but wide enough to cover realistic orbit family ranges. To refine later you'd compute the actual C range spanned by the orbit families in your HDF5 data and fit σ_C to that spread per libration point (they'd each get their own σ).

**CR3BP metric(s):**
Our neural ODE is learning to predict the CR3BP acceleration vector at any point in p-hase space. Given a state $[x, y, z, v_x, v_y, v_z]$ in the rotating frame, it ouputs $[a_x, a_y, a_z]$. The model is trying to approximate the true acceleration. Our precious metric was mean acceleration error averaged over all trajectory points in the test dataset (e.g. we take our test orbits and at each timestep for each orbit, we calculate the absolute acceleration percent error, then average over all of these points). This isn't the best metric because it isn't weighted by scientific importance - we might care a lot how the model performs near L1 and L2, but we don't care as much near L3 since we don't have missions there. 

Idea for a metric - rather than sampling along test trajectories, place a fixed grid of points across the entire cislunar phase space and evaluate the acceleration error at each one. Then, weight each error by how much we care about each location.
$E(x) = w_{\text{region}}(x) * w_\text{manifold}(x) * ||a_\text{true}(x) - a_\text{model}(x)||$
Terms:
- $w_{\text{region}}(x)$  - this tells us how scientifically important the region we are in is.
$$
w_{\mathrm{region}}(\mathbf{x})
=
\sum_{i=1}^{5}
\alpha_i
\exp\!\left(
-\frac{d(\mathbf{x},L_i)}{\sigma}
\right),

$$

	where $\alpha_i$ is the weighting factor for each Lagrange points (this sets the scientific value), and $\sigma$ defines a radius of influence. We use this because the lagrange points have differently sized dynamical neighborhoods. Example - L1/L2 neighborhoods are much smaller (~.15 canonical units) than L4/L5.
The final scalar is the weighted mean of $E(x)$ across all grid points. By formulating the error this way, we are able to both visualize where in phase space the error is the highest, as well as report a single scalar.

How do we choose velocities? We don't just place position points, we need full 6D states `[x, y, z, vx, vy, vz]`. The way we get the velocity component is: at each grid position, we pick a target Jacobi constant value `C`, then compute what speed is required: v² = 2Ω(x,y) - C where `Ω` is the effective potential at that position. If `v² > 0` the point is accessible and we pick a velocity direction (uniformly sampled in the xy-plane) to get a full state. If `v² ≤ 0` the point is in the forbidden region for that `C` and we skip it. Once we know the required speed at a position, we need to choose a direcition. In 3D, we have an entire sphere of possible velocity vectors, all consistent with the same Jacobi constant at that position. For the 2D case, we evenly sample $n_{vel}$ angles around 0 to 2$\pi$.

Most of the expensive computation doesn't depend on the model at all — it only depends on the CR3BP physics and the grid geometry, which are fixed. So we compute those once and save them.

What we precompute:
- The grid states `[x, y, z, vx, vy, vz]` — the 14,684 points spread across the cislunar phase space. Fixed forever, never changes.
- `a_true` at every grid point — the true CR3BP acceleration at each state, computed from the closed-form equations of motion. This is also fixed — the true dynamics don't change.
-  `w_region` at every grid point — the libration point proximity weights. These depend only on position and the libration point geometry, not on the model.
- `w_manifold` at every grid point (optional) — the manifold proximity weights. These depend only on the precomputed manifold arcs from `mlds-astro`, not on the model.
All of this gets saved to a single HDF5 file.

What happens at evaluation time:
The only thing that changes between model evaluations is `a_model` — the model's predicted acceleration at each grid point. So at evaluation time you just:
1. Load the cached grid
2. Run the model at all 14,684 states in one batched forward pass → get `a_model`
3. Compute `||a_true - a_model||` at each point
4. Multiply by the precomputed weights
5. Take the weighted mean → scalar metric

Steps 1 and 3-5 are essentially free. Step 2 is the only real cost, and it's just one forward pass through the network — very fast regardless of grid size.

**On intelligent gridding:**

The key insight is that you don't need to evaluate on a dense uniform grid — you need **coverage of the important regions** without wasting evaluations in unimportant ones. A few strategies:

**Stratified sampling by Jacobi constant** Rather than a uniform 6D grid (which is intractable), fix a set of C values spanning the cislunar energy range and sample position/velocity pairs consistent with each C level. This keeps you on physically meaningful surfaces and naturally constrains the sampling.

**Importance-weighted sampling density** Sample more points near L1/L2 and fewer near L3/far cislunar — directly matching the `w_region` weighting. Points that will have low weight anyway don't need dense coverage.

What is an invariant manifold? For a dynamical system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ with a flow map $\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)$, an invariant manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is a set of states that maps into itself under the dynamics $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathcal{M} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) \in \mathcal{M}$. In CR3BP trajectory design, these manifolds are usually associated with an equilibrium point or a periodic orbit like Lyapunov, halo, NRHO orbits. **In plain terms, an invariant manifold is a geometric set in phase space that the dynamics cannot leave.**

The manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is invariant if starting on it means the dynamics keeps you on it: $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathcal{M} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) \in \mathcal{M}.$

In words, if you start on the manifold you will stay there.

What is a flow map? A flow map is the oeprator that takes an initial state and returns the state reached after evolving under the dynamics for some time - basically it is representing propagation of an initial condition under specified dynamics. In words: $\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) = \text{the point reached by propagating } \mathbf{x}_0 \text{ forward by time } t$, e.g. it is the exact nonlinear propagation operator for the dynamical system.

The STM is related but not the same object. The flow map is nonlinear in general $\mathbf{x}(t) = \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)$, while the STM is the local linearization of that flow map with respect to the initial condition $\mathbf{\Phi}(t,0) = \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}(t)}{\partial \mathbf{x}_0} = \frac{\partial \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)}{\partial \mathbf{x}_0}$. So, the STM is the Jacobian of the flow map with repsect to the initial state. The flow map tells you where a particular initial condition goes, but a STM tells you how a small perturbation to that initial condition evolves. 

> [!note]- Gradient vs Jacobian
> The gradient is the vector formed by the partial derivatives of a _scalar_ function.
> 
> The Jacobian matrix is the matrix formed by the partial derivatives of a _vector_ function. Its vectors are the gradients of the respective components of the function.
> 
> E.g., with some argument omissions,
> 
> ∇𝑓(𝑥,𝑦)=(𝑓′𝑥𝑓′𝑦)
> 
> 𝐽(𝑓(𝑥,𝑦),𝑔(𝑥,𝑦))=(𝑓′𝑥𝑓′𝑦𝑔′𝑥𝑔′𝑦)=(∇𝑓;∇𝑔).
> 
> If you want, the Jacobian is a generalization of the gradient to vector functions

> [!note]- Monodromy matrix as it relates to stable and unstable manifolds
> For a periodic orbit, the stable and unstable manifolds are generated from the linearized dynamics around the orbit. You compute the monodromy matrix, which is the state transition matrix over one full period:
> 
> \mathbf{\Phi}(T,0).
> 
> Its eigenvalues and eigenvectors describe the local behavior near the periodic orbit. If the periodic orbit has one real eigenvalue outside the unit circle,
> 
> |\lambda_u| > 1,
> 
> then the corresponding eigenvector gives the local unstable direction. Perturbing the periodic orbit slightly along that direction and propagating forward produces the unstable manifold.
> 
> If it has one real eigenvalue inside the unit circle,
> 
> |\lambda_s| < 1,
> 
> then the corresponding eigenvector gives the local stable direction. Perturbing along that direction and propagating backward is a common way to trace out the stable manifold. Forward in time, those trajectories approach the periodic orbit.
> 
> A quasi-stable manifold is a less universal term. It is not as mathematically standard as stable or unstable manifold. In astrodynamics, people may use “quasi-stable” to describe trajectories or structures that remain near a region for a long time but are not truly asymptotically stable.
> 
> That distinction matters. A true stable manifold has an asymptotic property:
> 
> \operatorname{dist}\left(\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0), \gamma\right)
> \to 0
> \quad \text{as} \quad t \to +\infty.
> 
> A quasi-stable trajectory may only satisfy something weaker, such as:
> 
> \operatorname{dist}\left(\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0), \gamma\right)
> \text{ remains small for a long but finite time.}
> 
> So “quasi-stable” usually means “temporarily stable-looking” or “weakly escaping,” not truly stable in the invariant-manifold sense.
> Stable and unstable manifolds are both kinds of invariant manifolds.

A **Hamiltonian system** describes dynamics in terms of an energy-like scalar function called the **Hamiltonian**:

$$
H(q,p)
$$

where $q$ represents generalized position coordinates and $p$ represents generalized momentum coordinates.

For many mechanical systems, the Hamiltonian is the total mechanical energy:

$$
H(q,p) = T(p) + V(q),
$$

so

$$
\text{Hamiltonian} = \text{kinetic energy} + \text{potential energy}.
$$

Instead of saying “forces cause accelerations,” Hamiltonian mechanics says that the **energy landscape determines how position and momentum evolve through phase space**.

The equations of motion are Hamilton’s equations:

$$
\dot{q} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p},
\qquad
\dot{p} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}.
$$

The first equation says that the way energy changes with momentum determines how position evolves. The second says that the way energy changes with position determines how momentum evolves, with a minus sign. That minus sign is what gives Hamiltonian motion its characteristic circulating behavior in phase space.

For a simple mass-spring system,

$$
H(q,p)
=
\frac{p^2}{2m}
+
\frac{1}{2}kq^2.
$$

The system trades energy between kinetic and potential energy. When the mass is far from equilibrium, energy is mostly potential. When it passes through equilibrium, energy is mostly kinetic. In phase space $(q,p)$, the trajectory forms closed loops because total energy is conserved.

So a useful intuition is that Hamiltonian motion follows contours of constant total energy in phase space

It does not usually move “downhill” like a dissipative system. Instead, it moves **around** the energy landscape. That is why Hamiltonian systems are strongly associated with conservative dynamics, oscillators, orbital motion, and phase-space structure.

For example, in orbital mechanics, the two-body problem can be written as a Hamiltonian system:

$$
H(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{p})
=
\frac{\|\mathbf{p}\|^2}{2m}
-
\frac{\mu m}{\|\mathbf{r}\|}.
$$

The first term is kinetic energy and the second term is gravitational potential energy. In the ideal two-body problem, the spacecraft does not spiral inward or outward because no energy is being added or removed. Its trajectory is constrained by the constant-energy structure of the system.

A useful contrast is a gradient system. A gradient system moves downhill on a scalar function:

$$
\dot{x} = -\nabla V(x).
$$

A Hamiltonian system instead moves perpendicular, in a structured sense, to the gradient of the Hamiltonian:

$$
\dot{x} = J \nabla H(x),
$$

where

$$
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
q \\
p
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
J =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & I \\
-I & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The matrix $J$ rotates the energy gradient into the direction of motion. That is why the system circulates around energy contours instead of descending them.

Intuitively, a Hamiltonian system is a conservative dynamical system where the total-energy function defines the geometry of motion. The state evolves through phase space in a way that preserves energy and follows structured position-momentum coupling.

**Hamiltonian in the CR3BP:**

In the circular restricted three-body problem, the Hamiltonian depends on the coordinate frame. The most common CR3BP formulation uses the **synodic rotating frame**, where the two primaries are fixed on the $x$-axis. In nondimensional units, the Hamiltonian is usually written as

$$
H(x,y,z,p_x,p_y,p_z)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2\right)
+
y p_x
-
x p_y
-
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
-
\frac{\mu}{r_2}.
$$

Here,

$$
r_1 = \sqrt{(x+\mu)^2+y^2+z^2},
$$

$$
r_2 = \sqrt{(x-1+\mu)^2+y^2+z^2},
$$

where $\mu$ is the nondimensional mass parameter,

$$
\mu = \frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2}.
$$

The larger primary is located at

$$
(-\mu,0,0),
$$

and the smaller primary is located at

$$
(1-\mu,0,0).
$$

The canonical momenta are not simply the velocities in the rotating frame. They are

$$
p_x = \dot{x} - y,
$$

$$
p_y = \dot{y} + x,
$$

$$
p_z = \dot{z}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\dot{x} = p_x + y,
$$

$$
\dot{y} = p_y - x,
$$

$$
\dot{z} = p_z.
$$

The Hamiltonian can also be written in terms of the rotating-frame velocities as

$$
H
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2+\dot{z}^2\right)
-
\frac{1}{2}(x^2+y^2)
-
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
-
\frac{\mu}{r_2}.
$$

This form makes the connection to the Jacobi constant clearer. Define the pseudo-potential

$$
\Omega(x,y,z)
=
\frac{1}{2}(x^2+y^2)
+
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
+
\frac{\mu}{r_2}.
$$

Then the CR3BP equations of motion are often written as

$$
\ddot{x} - 2\dot{y} = \frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial x},
$$

$$
\ddot{y} + 2\dot{x} = \frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial y},
$$

$$
\ddot{z} = \frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial z}.
$$

The Jacobi constant is

$$
C
=
2\Omega
-
\left(\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2+\dot{z}^2\right).
$$

Using the velocity-form Hamiltonian above,

$$
H
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2+\dot{z}^2\right)
-
\Omega,
$$

so

$$
C = -2H.
$$

Intuitively, the CR3BP Hamiltonian is the conserved energy-like quantity in the rotating frame. It combines kinetic energy, gravitational potential from both primaries, and the apparent potential associated with the rotating frame. The extra canonical terms

$$
y p_x - x p_y
$$

appear because the rotating frame introduces Coriolis-type coupling between position and momentum.

So the compact answer is:

$$
\boxed{
H
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2\right)
+
y p_x
-
x p_y
-
\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
-
\frac{\mu}{r_2}
}
$$

with

$$
\boxed{
C=-2H.
}
$$

What is a Hessian? It is the square matrix of all the second-order partial derivatives of a multivariable function. For a scalar function $f(\mathbf{x})$, where $\mathbf{x} = [x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n]^T$, the Hessian is 
$$
\nabla^2 f(\mathbf{x})
=
\mathbf{H}_f(\mathbf{x})
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} &
\cdots &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_n} \\

\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_1} &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} &
\cdots &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_n} \\

\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\

\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_1} &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_2} &
\cdots &
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2}
\end{bmatrix}
$$
