## Sundman Transform
**What is it?**
Sundman transform is a state-dependent reparameterization of time. The reason we do this is because physical time may not be the best independent variable for analysis or computation. What does this mean? Many dynamical systems evolve very nonuniformly in physical time. Some regions of the trajectory may have rapdily changing dynamics, while others evolve slowly. If you integrate uniformly in $t$, you may either undersample fast regions or waster resolution in slow regions. A Sundman transform lets you redistribute the independent variable so taht the numerical problem is better behaved. 

How does this relate to 2BP dynamics? The most common reason for using the transform is regularization. In 2BP, our gravitational equations can become nearly singular when $r$ becomes small. Take 2BP dynamics:
$$\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -\frac{\mu}{r^3}\mathbf{r}.$$
As $r \rightarrow 0$, the right-hand side becomes singular. A Sundman-type time scaling such as $dt = r\, ds$ slows physical time near small $r$. So when $r$ is small, a fixed step in fictitious time $s$ corresponds to a small step in physical time $t$. This gives the integration more resolution near the problematic region. In celestial mechanics, this is a standard ingredient of regularization methods often combined with coordinate changes like Levi-Civita regularization in 2D or Kustaanheimo-Stiefel regularization in 3D. 

Basically, we are choosing a fictitious time scale to integrae such that difficult parts of motion are stretched out. We're not actually transforming time or saying it's physically different. We are choosing that instead of using physical time as the independent variable of integration, we will use a new paramter (the fictitious time) and we will track how physical time advances along that parameter. 

**The Math**
Our orignal ODE is written in physical time:
$$\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},t).$$
A Sundman transform introduces a new independent variable (fictitious time, typically $s$ or $\tau$) through
$$dt = g(\mathbf{x},t)\,ds,$$
or equivalently 
$$\frac{dt}{ds}=g(\mathbf{x},t).$$
Our dynamics are expressed as 
$$\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{ds} = \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}\frac{dt}{ds} = g(\mathbf{x},t)\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},t).$$

So we are actually choosing $g(\mathbf{x},t)$ to make our problem more tractable. We must be careful and note that a Sundman tranform changes the vector field. If our original system is $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ and the transformed system is $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{ds} = g(\mathbf{x})\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$, these two equations are not equivalent and only have the same state-space trajectories if $g(\mathbf{x})>0$ and you keep track of the time mapping $\frac{dt}{ds}=g(\mathbf{x})$. This matters for neural ODEs because if we train a modle using physical time, the target vector field is $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}$. If you train using Sundman time, the target vector field is $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{ds}$. Those differ by the factor $g(\mathbf{x})$. So a Sundman transform is not just normalization. Normalization changes units; a Sundman transform changes the independent variable and therefore changes the derivative that the model is learning. In the transformed system, we can think of the augmented dynamics as 
$$\frac{d}{ds} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}\\ t \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} g(\mathbf{x},t)\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},t)\\ g(\mathbf{x},t) \end{bmatrix}.$$

How does this relate to 2BP? In 2BP, a common choice for $\frac{dt}{ds}=g(\mathbf{x},t)$ is $\frac{dt}{ds}=r$. Then near periapsis, $r$ is smaller, so physical time advances slowly per unit $s$. That means the integrator gets more resolution in the part of the orbit where the state changes rapidly in physical time. The physical trajectory is the same. The orbit in state space is not changed. What changes is the **parametrization** of that trajectory. In physical time, $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}(t)$. In Sundman time, $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}(s), \qquad t=t(s)$. The two descriptions are connected by $\mathbf{x}(s)=\mathbf{x}(t(s))$.

## Why your `dt` went pathologically small — this is the real bug candidate

Here's the mechanism, and it's a genuine, structural risk in this design, not just a fluke:

Your normalization already sets `μ_char = 1` (standard `Normalization2BP` scaling: `l_char`, `v_char = sqrt(μ/l_char)`, `t_char = sqrt(l_char³/μ)`). Under the Sundman relation `dt = r^k ds`, near periapsis in a _normalized_ system, `r*` (dimensionless, normalized radius) can be **less than 1** — e.g., for an eccentric orbit with `r_peri* ≈ 0.1–0.3` in Earth-radii-normalized units. So:

```
ds = dt / r*^k
```

If the solver's step controller (`dt0`, `rtol`/`atol` in `default_2BP.yaml`) is tuned in the original _physical-time_ sense (i.e., calibrated for how fast `t` changes), but the arrays are now indexed in `s`, the _apparent_ `dt` seen by the solver's adaptive controller is really `ds`, and — critically — the scaling direction is the opposite of what you might expect:

- Sundman's whole point is that fictitious time `s` moves **slowly** through periapsis relative to real time `t` when `k=1`, i.e. `ds = dt/r` is _small_ when `r` is small and `dt` fixed. But if you're sampling _uniformly in s_ (which is what `calculate_orbit_from_E` does — uniform in E, hence uniform in `s`), then the _physical_ time steps `dt = r^k · ds` become very small near periapsis (since `r` is small there and `ds` is fixed/uniform).

So concretely: **you sample uniformly in Sundman time `s`, meaning the actual `dt` values your dataset's own timestamps represent are tiny near periapsis** — that's not a bug, that's the transform doing exactly what it's designed to do (clustering resolution at periapsis). The failure mode is likely one of:

1. **Mismatch between what space the solver's `dt0`/tolerances are tuned for vs. what's actually being integrated.** If `default_2BP.yaml`'s `rtol`/`atol`/`dt0` were tuned assuming physical-time dynamics, but the model is now solving in `s`-space with `dx/ds = r*·f(x)` — a _rescaled_ vector field — the step controller may misjudge appropriate step sizes, especially since `r*·f(x)` can itself be numerically much flatter or steeper than `f(x)` depending on `k` and the normalization.
    
2. **The `l_char` mismatch trap the stash comments explicitly warn about.** If `l_char` used in `sampling_2BP.py`'s data generation ever drifts out of sync with the `l_char` passed to `Normalization2BP` at training time (e.g., you regenerate data or change a config default independently), the stored Sundman time axis silently stops being the clean nondimensional `s` — segments/timesteps become inconsistent in scale across orbits, and segment-length-based training (`segment_length_strategy: [[18, 18]]`) would then be handed to the solver as wildly inconsistent physical `dt` per segment, since 18 samples of `s` no longer maps predictably to `Δt`.
    
3. **`dt0=ts[1]-ts[0]`** in `NeuralODE.solve` ([neuralODE.py](vscode-webview://0mmdnoli9kst5h0s6gbu32skhnvkupdq2rkbemh8csr7snlld696/neuralODE/neuralODE.py)) is computed directly from consecutive dataset timestamps. If those timestamps are Sundman-time (uniform in `s` by construction) but the _comment_ in `default_2BP.yaml`'s config (`# sundman_time: true # set true when using a dataset generated with use_sundman=True`) wasn't actually flipped on, or the reverse — dataset is plain physical time but `sundman_time: true` — the network output gets an extra unwanted `r*` scaling on top of already-Kepler-nonuniform time steps, compounding instability, not fixing it.
    

Given this is exactly the state you described ("not sure everything was implemented correctly"), my strong suspicion is **#2 or #3** — a bookkeeping mismatch between what the dataset actually encodes (Sundman `s` vs. physical `t`) and what `sundman_time` in the training config assumes, rather than the Sundman transform being conceptually wrong. That's very fixable, but needs checking against the actual dataset that was used.

re: del f/ del theta behavior: 
Additionally, the term we call the vector-field parameter sensitivity, ∂f(z(t), t, θ)/∂θ, is increased near periapsis where dynamics are largest, so it is not surprising that the model places more parametric effort there. However, this means that gradient descent steps are disproportionately shaped by periapsis error, and the model is implicitly optimizing harder for periapsis accuracy than apoapsis accuracy even before any adjoint decay is considered. Combined with the adjoint being largest at the start of each segment and decaying within it, both terms in the integrand are simultaneously large near periapsis when a segment begins there and small near apoapsis. Thus, the parameter update dL/dθ is doubly concentrated at periapsis when considering the effects of the adjoint, and the training signal near apoapsis is weak regardless of where errors actually occur in the orbit.

It is an open question whether the concentration of parametric effort near periapsis is desirable or problematic. One perspective is that ∂f/∂θ being large at periapsis is physically motivated: the acceleration field is steepest there, so the network necessarily needs high output sensitivity to weights in that region to fit the dynamics accurately. The opposing perspective is that the network has a finite parameter budget, and if ∂f/∂θ is heavily concentrated at periapsis, the weights are being allocated disproportionately toward periapsis accuracy at the expense of apoapsis. Under this view, we are not fighting the physics by asking for a more uniform ∂f/∂θ but rather asking the network to distribute its representational capacity more evenly across the orbit, which is what we actually want for accurate long-horizon propagation. We do not currently have a definitive answer on which perspective is correct, and this remains an open question for future investigation.

TLDR; not sure yet, we might want to run some tests and see if results improve

The loss itself (percent_error_loss, in `kick_at` in AdjointDiagnosticExperiment.py) is already relative error normalized by ||y_i||, with each timestep weighted equally (1/n_terms). So the loss's intent is uniform relative accuracy across the whole orbit, periapsis and apoapsis alike. The finding is that even though the loss is uniform, the gradient dL/dθ ends up periapsis-concentrated anyway, for two separate reasons: large ∂f/∂θ there, and adjoint decay biasing toward segment starts. So there's a real gap between what the loss asks for and what the optimizer actually does with it.

The ∂f/∂θ concentration is the genuinely ambiguous piece, but it's testable rather than philosophical: bin the trained model's actual test error by true anomaly or radius. If apoapsis error is higher than periapsis error despite apoapsis dynamics being slower and easier to fit, that's evidence the network really is undertrained there because of the gradient concentration, not because the problem is harder. If apoapsis error is comparable or better, the concentration is probably fine — the network needed the extra capacity at periapsis and got it without starving apoapsis.

**Where it deviates from the doc — §7's augmented-time tracking:**

The doc is explicit that if training targets are indexed by physical (nondimensional) time `τ`, you need to track `τ` alongside the state via an augmented ODE `dτ/ds = g(x*)`, because otherwise the model may be phase-correct in `s` but wrong in `τ`. Looking at `sampling_2BP.py:134-140`, when `use_sundman=True`, the **dataset's own time axis is stored as Sundman `τ`, not physical time** — `times = √(a/μ)·E_unwrapped`. So the "labels" are already indexed by the Sundman variable, not physical nondimensional time.

That actually sidesteps the problem the doc is warning about, in one specific case: since data is uniformly sampled in eccentric anomaly `E`, and `τ = √(a/μ)·E` is exactly linear in `E` for k=1, "uniform in E" is already "uniform in τ" — no augmented-time integration is needed for _this_ sampling scheme, which is a nice special case. But it also means the current code's `sundman_time` config flag conflates two different things the doc treats as separate: whether the _stored dataset time axis_ is `τ` (a data-generation choice in `sampling_2BP.py`) versus whether the _solver_ is integrating in `s` and multiplying `f_θ` by `g(x*)` (a training-time choice in `neuralODE.py`). If someone trains with `sundman_time=True` in `neuralODE.py` against a dataset generated with `use_sundman=False` (physical time), the model would be integrated as if `ts` were the Sundman variable `s`, but the targets would actually be spaced in physical time — a real phase mismatch, exactly the failure mode §7 warns about.

**Bottom line:** the _mechanism_ is implemented correctly per the doc (normalize → then apply Sundman multiplicatively on already-normalized state), but there's no code-level guard tying `Func.sundman_time` (training-time behavior) to the dataset's `use_sundman` metadata flag (data-generation-time choice) — that consistency is currently left to the user to get right via config, not enforced. Want me to add a check (e.g., in `NeuralODE.__init__` or the data loader) that raises if `config.parameters.sundman_time` doesn't match the dataset's `metadata["sundman_time"]`?

# Normalization vs. Sundman Time for a Neural ODE Learning 2BP Dynamics

For a neural ODE learning 2BP dynamics, it makes sense to use the classic 2BP nondimensionalization first. That gives

$$
\mu^* = 1
$$

and puts position, velocity, and time on more comparable numerical scales.

A Sundman transform is a separate operation. It does **not** replace nondimensionalization. It changes the independent variable used to integrate the ODE.

So the clean workflow is:

$$
\text{dimensional 2BP}
\rightarrow
\text{classic nondimensionalization with } \mu^*=1
\rightarrow
\text{optional Sundman time reparameterization}.
$$

## 1. Start with dimensional 2BP

The dimensional two-body equations are

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{v},
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}
=
-\mu \frac{\mathbf{r}}{r^3}.
$$

Here:

- $\mathbf{r}$ is dimensional position
- $\mathbf{v}$ is dimensional velocity
- $t$ is dimensional physical time
- $\mu$ is the dimensional gravitational parameter
- $r = \|\mathbf{r}\|$

## 2. Apply classic 2BP nondimensionalization

Choose a characteristic length scale $l^*$ and define the characteristic time scale

$$
t^*
=
\sqrt{\frac{(l^*)^3}{\mu}}.
$$

Then define

$$
\mathbf{r}
=
l^* \mathbf{r}^*,
$$

$$
t
=
t^* \tau,
$$

$$
\mathbf{v}
=
\frac{l^*}{t^*}\mathbf{v}^*.
$$

The nondimensional velocity scale is therefore

$$
v^*
=
\frac{l^*}{t^*}.
$$

After substituting into the 2BP equations, the nondimensional dynamics become

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}^*}{d\tau}
=
\mathbf{v}^*,
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{d\tau}
=
-\frac{\mathbf{r}^*}{(r^*)^3}.
$$

The effective nondimensional gravitational parameter is now

$$
\mu^* = 1.
$$

This is the normalization you already want for the neural ODE. It removes large dimensional constants and makes the numerical scales of position, velocity, and time more reasonable.

The key point is that the nondimensional time variable $\tau$ is still physical time, just rescaled by a constant:

$$
t = t^*\tau.
$$

So $\tau$ is a constant-scaled physical clock.

## 3. Apply the Sundman transform after nondimensionalization

A Sundman transform introduces a new independent variable, usually called $s$, related to nondimensional time $\tau$ by

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*),
$$

where

$$
g(\mathbf{x}^*) > 0.
$$

For 2BP, a common radius-based choice is

$$
g(\mathbf{x}^*) = (r^*)^\alpha.
$$

Then

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
(r^*)^\alpha.
$$

Using the chain rule,

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{d\tau}
\frac{d\tau}{ds}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*)\mathbf{f}^*(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

For 2BP,

$$
\mathbf{x}^*
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{r}^* \\
\mathbf{v}^*
\end{bmatrix},
$$

so the Sundman-transformed nondimensional equations are

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}^*}{ds}
=
(r^*)^\alpha \mathbf{v}^*,
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{ds}
=
-(r^*)^\alpha
\frac{\mathbf{r}^*}{(r^*)^3}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{ds}
=
-\frac{\mathbf{r}^*}{(r^*)^{3-\alpha}}.
$$

## 4. What normalization does vs. what Sundman time does

Normalization answers:

$$
\text{How do I scale position, velocity, and time so the numerical values are order-one?}
$$

Sundman time answers:

$$
\text{What independent variable should the ODE be integrated with respect to?}
$$

They are not the same thing.

The normalization time scale $t^*$ is constant:

$$
t = t^*\tau.
$$

The Sundman transform is state-dependent:

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

So normalization changes the units. Sundman changes the clock.

## 5. Why this may help neural ODE training

In physical time, 2BP motion is not uniform around an eccentric orbit.

Near periapsis:

- $r$ is small
- velocity is large
- acceleration is large
- the state changes rapidly

Near apoapsis:

- $r$ is large
- velocity is smaller
- acceleration is weaker
- the state changes more slowly

The physical-time acceleration magnitude scales like

$$
\left\|
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{d\tau}
\right\|
\sim
\frac{1}{(r^*)^2}.
$$

After a Sundman transform with

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds} = (r^*)^\alpha,
$$

the transformed acceleration-like derivative scales as

$$
\left\|
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{ds}
\right\|
\sim
\frac{1}{(r^*)^{2-\alpha}}.
$$

So:

### No Sundman transform

If

$$
\alpha = 0,
$$

then

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds} = 1,
$$

and

$$
\left\|
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{ds}
\right\|
\sim
\frac{1}{(r^*)^2}.
$$

This is just the original nondimensional physical-time system.

### Moderate Sundman transform

If

$$
\alpha = 1,
$$

then

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds} = r^*,
$$

and

$$
\left\|
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{ds}
\right\|
\sim
\frac{1}{r^*}.
$$

This reduces the radial dynamic range of the acceleration-like term.

### Stronger Sundman transform

If

$$
\alpha = 2,
$$

then

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds} = (r^*)^2,
$$

and

$$
\left\|
\frac{d\mathbf{v}^*}{ds}
\right\|
\sim
1.
$$

This roughly flattens the acceleration magnitude with respect to radius.

However, the position derivative becomes

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}^*}{ds}
=
(r^*)^2\mathbf{v}^*,
$$

which may introduce a different scaling issue.

For that reason, $\alpha=1$ is usually the cleaner first experiment.

## 6. Recommended neural ODE formulation

Let the neural network represent the normalized physical-time vector field:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{d\tau}
=
\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

Then apply Sundman time as a solver-level wrapper:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*)\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

For example,

$$
g(\mathbf{x}^*) = r^*.
$$

This is useful because the neural network still represents the physical nondimensional dynamics, while Sundman time only changes how the solver marches along the trajectory.

In other words, the network learns

$$
\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*)
\approx
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{v}^* \\
-\frac{\mathbf{r}^*}{(r^*)^3}
\end{bmatrix},
$$

but the integrator advances according to

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
r^*\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

## 7. Important: track physical time if your labels are physical-time samples

If your training data are indexed by nondimensional physical time,

$$
\mathbf{x}^*(\tau_k),
$$

then you should also track $\tau$ while integrating in $s$.

Use the augmented system

$$
\frac{d}{ds}
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{x}^* \\
\tau
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
g(\mathbf{x}^*)\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*) \\
g(\mathbf{x}^*)
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

That is,

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*)\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*),
$$

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

This matters because the Sundman transform changes the clock. If you compare the predicted state to a target at the wrong physical time, the orbit may look geometrically correct but have the wrong phase.

## 8. Should you apply Sundman before or after normalization?

For a neural ODE, apply normalization first.

The recommended order is

$$
\boxed{
\text{normalize first, then apply Sundman time}
}
$$

or more explicitly,

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{x}
\rightarrow
\mathbf{x}^*
\quad
\text{then}
\quad
\tau
\rightarrow
s.
}
$$

If you apply Sundman first in dimensional variables, for example

$$
\frac{dt}{ds} = r^\alpha,
$$

then $r^\alpha$ has dimensions. When you later nondimensionalize, extra scale factors involving $l^*$ and $t^*$ appear.

That is mathematically fixable, but it is easy to accidentally make the transformed learning problem depend on arbitrary scale choices.

By applying Sundman after nondimensionalization, the Sundman factor is clean and dimensionless:

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
(r^*)^\alpha.
$$

No hidden $\mu$, no hidden $l^*$, no hidden $t^*$.

## 9. Practical experiment

Compare the following cases using the same physical-time rollout metrics:

### Baseline

$$
\alpha = 0
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{d\tau}
=
\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

### Sundman with $g=r^*$

$$
\alpha = 1
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
r^*\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*),
$$

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
r^*.
$$

### Sundman with $g=(r^*)^2$

$$
\alpha = 2
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
(r^*)^2\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*),
$$

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
(r^*)^2.
$$

Evaluate all models against the same physical-time quantities:

- state rollout error at fixed $\tau$
- acceleration-field error
- energy error
- phase error
- long-horizon orbital drift

## 10. Main takeaway

Use classic 2BP nondimensionalization first:

$$
\mu^* = 1.
$$

Then, if desired, apply a Sundman transform in nondimensional variables:

$$
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*).
$$

The clean neural ODE formulation is

$$
\boxed{
\frac{d\mathbf{x}^*}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*)\mathbf{f}_\theta(\mathbf{x}^*)
}
$$

with

$$
\boxed{
\frac{d\tau}{ds}
=
g(\mathbf{x}^*)
}
$$

if your training targets are indexed by physical nondimensional time.

So the short answer is:

$$
\boxed{
\text{Yes, use them together. Normalize first. Apply Sundman after.}
}
$$

Changes from original ref:
he adjoint method (Chen 2018, eq. 4-5) defines three quantities that evolve backwards in time:

- **z(t)** — the hidden state (your ODE trajectory)
- **a(t) = dL/dz(t)** — the adjoint, measuring how the loss changes with the state at each time
- **dL/dθ** — the gradient w.r.t. network parameters, accumulated over time

Their dynamics (eq. 4) are:

```
dz/dt    =  f(z, t, θ)
da/dt    = -a(t)ᵀ · ∂f/∂z
dL/dθ   += -a(t)ᵀ · ∂f/∂θ
```

The key insight is `∂f/∂z` and `∂f/∂θ` don't need to be formed as full matrices — you only need the product of `a(t)` with them, which is exactly `grad((f · a).sum())`.

**What the original code did:**

The original `backward_dynamics` called `autograd.grad(f, [z, θ], grad_outputs=a)` — one call per sample in the batch. This is O(B) sequential Python calls, one gradient computation at a time.

**What our version does:**

```python
grad_fn = grad(lambda p_dict, h, a: (f_single(p_dict, h) * a).sum(), argnums=(0, 1))
batched_grad = vmap(grad_fn, in_dims=(None, 0, 0))
param_grads_batch, grad_ht = batched_grad(param_dict, ht.detach(), at)
```

`grad` of `(f · a).sum()` with respect to `(θ, z)` gives exactly `∂f/∂θ · a` and `∂f/∂z · a` — the two terms from eq. 4 — for a single sample. `vmap` then vectorizes this across the whole batch simultaneously.

**The jump condition fix:**

The original forward pass only saved the final state `output = state[1]`, and the backward pass started the adjoint solve from there with a single `output_gradients` term. But Figure 2 of Chen 2018 shows that at each observation time tᵢ, the adjoint receives a "kick":

```
a(tᵢ⁻) = a(tᵢ⁺) + dL/dz(tᵢ)
```

Our version saves the full trajectory, then in the backward loop:

```python
at = state[2] + output_gradients[i]  # jump condition at each timestep
```

Without this, gradients from intermediate timesteps are ignored — you're only backpropping through the final prediction, not the whole trajectory.

**Eq. 4:** `da/dt = -a(t)ᵀ · ∂f/∂z`

The right-hand side is a matrix-vector product: the Jacobian `∂f/∂z` (shape `state_dim × state_dim`) transposed and multiplied by `a(t)` (shape `state_dim`). The result is a vector of the same shape as `a`.

Now, consider the scalar function `g(z) = (f(z) · a).sum() = Σᵢ fᵢ(z) · aᵢ`. Its gradient with respect to `z` is:

```
∂g/∂z = (∂f/∂z)ᵀ · a
```

which is exactly the right-hand side of eq. 4 (without the negative sign). So `grad((f · a).sum(), z)` gives you `(∂f/∂z)ᵀ · a` — the adjoint dynamics term — without forming the full Jacobian.

**Eq. 5:** `dL/dθ = ∫ a(t)ᵀ · ∂f/∂θ dt`

Same idea — `grad((f · a).sum(), θ)` gives `(∂f/∂θ)ᵀ · a`, the integrand of eq. 5, accumulated over the backward solve.

**On the sign:** you're right to be suspicious. Eq. 4 has a negative sign, but `backward_dynamics` returns `grad_ht` without negation, and `delta_t[i]` is positive. So the RK4 step moves in the wrong direction for the adjoint.

However — looking at the original repo's code, this appears to be a pre-existing issue inherited from the upstream, not something we introduced. Our change was purely replacing the per-sample loop with `vmap`; the sign behavior is unchanged from the original. Worth flagging as a potential bug to investigate separately though.

To recap what we care about:

We have a neural ODE where the state evolves as $\dot{z}(t) = f(z(t), t, \theta)$, integrated from $t_0$ to $t_1$, giving $z(t_1)$. The loss $L$ compares $z(t_1)$ to a target. We want $\frac{dL}{d\theta}$ to update $\theta$ by gradient descent. The adjoint $a(t) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z(t)}$ is a vector of the same dimension as the state (6 in our case). 

The dynamics of the adjoint are given by another ODE, which can be thought of as an instantaneous analog of the chain rule: $$
\frac{d\mathbf{a}(t)}{dt}
=
-\mathbf{a}(t)^\top
\frac{\partial f(\mathbf{z}(t), t, \theta)}{\partial \mathbf{z}}
\tag{4}
$$

The loss is updated according to $$
\frac{dL}{d\theta}
=
-\int_{t_1}^{t_0}
\mathbf{a}(t)^\top
\frac{\partial f(\mathbf{z}(t), t, \theta)}{\partial \theta}
\, dt
\tag{5}
$$

What is the adjoint telling us? We thought what it was saying how is the loss related to the state. This isn't the full picture. the adjoint is not correleated with the sate - it is the gradient of the loss with respect to a hypothetical perturbation of the trajectory at time t. It informs us which states, times, and vector-field evaluations contribute to the loss which then informs our gradient updates for how we should change our neural network weights to reduce the loss. 

In our forward pass, we start from an initial condition $\mathbf{z}_0^{(i)}$ then integrate the leawrned dynamics forward over the same time grid as the truth data, giving us $\hat{\mathbf{z}}^{(i)}(t_0), \hat{\mathbf{z}}^{(i)}(t_1), \dots, \hat{\mathbf{z}}^{(i)}(t_N)$. The training dataset gives the corresponding true states as $\mathbf{z}^{(i)}(t_0), \mathbf{z}^{(i)}(t_1), \dots, \mathbf{z}^{(i)}(t_N)$. At each saved time, the trajectory error is $\mathbf{e}^{(i)}(t_k) = \hat{\mathbf{z}}^{(i)}(t_k) - \mathbf{z}^{(i)}(t_k)$.  We use mean percent error: $\ell^{(i)} = \frac{100}{N+1} \sum_{k=0}^{N} \frac{ \left\| \hat{\mathbf{z}}^{(i)}(t_k) - \mathbf{z}^{(i)}(t_k) \right\| }{ \left\| \mathbf{z}^{(i)}(t_k) \right\| }$ and average over our batch of orbits: $L = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} \ell^{(i)}$. Thus, we get on scalar representing batch-level trajectory mismatch. So, in our forward pass: select orbit trajectories and their ICs --> integrate the neural ODE forward --> compare true and predicted trjactory and compute loss. 

Adjoint is sensitivity of the loss with respect to the predicted state. It asks - if I slightly perturb the predicted state here, how much does the total loss change? So, a point along the trajectory could have a large state error but relatively small adjoint if changing the state does not strongly affect the total loss, or a popint can have a modest state error but a large adjoint if small changes there strongly affect the rest of the trajectory. For 2BP, a small velocity error early in the orbit can become a large phase error later (so a small position error near periapsis can affect the acceleration strongly which will have a large effect on the later trajectory).

hen people say “the adjoint tells us the sensitivity to perturbations,” the perturbations are conceptual infinitesimal nudges in the state variables. In your case, the state variables are position and velocity. So the implied perturbations are tiny changes to x, y, z, v_x, v_y, or v_z. In implementation, you are not repeatedly rerunning the trajectory with slightly different positions and velocities. The adjoint method computes those sensitivities using derivatives of the loss and derivatives of the learned dynamics.

The process starts with the forward pass. You take an initial position and velocity, integrate the neural ODE forward, and obtain a predicted trajectory. Since your training data are position and velocity, the loss compares the predicted positions and velocities against the true 2BP positions and velocities at the saved time points. At this stage, the loss can already tell which direction would reduce the error at each saved time. For example, if the predicted x-position is too large, the loss locally “wants” that prediction to move in the negative x-direction. If the predicted v_y is too small, the loss locally “wants” v_y to increase. This first sensitivity comes directly from the loss function.

For a simple mean squared error loss, this immediate sensitivity is very intuitive: it points in the direction of the prediction error. For mean percent error, the idea is similar, except the sensitivity is scaled by the magnitude of the true state because of the percent-error normalization. Either way, the loss provides a direct signal at the trajectory points where you compare prediction and truth. It says, in effect, “given the predicted state and the true state at this time, here is how the predicted state should move to reduce the loss.”

The adjoint method then propagates that loss sensitivity backward through time. This is the part that makes it different from just looking at pointwise trajectory error. The predicted state at a later time depends on the predicted state at earlier times. If the velocity was slightly wrong early in the orbit, the position later may become wrong. If the learned acceleration was slightly wrong near periapsis, the outgoing velocity may be wrong, which can then create a phase error over the rest of the orbit. So the loss at a later time may have been caused by an error much earlier in the trajectory.

The adjoint calculation walks backward through the predicted trajectory and repeatedly asks: if the loss is sensitive to the state here, how sensitive must it have been to the state a little earlier, given the dynamics that moved the state from earlier to here? That backward transfer uses the local behavior of the learned vector field. In a 2BP neural ODE, the learned dynamics take the current position and velocity and return something like velocity and acceleration. The adjoint uses the sensitivity of that learned vector field to determine how a small change in an earlier position or velocity would have changed the later state, and therefore how it would have changed the loss.

So there are two main ingredients. The loss tells you the immediate correction direction at the output trajectory points. The dynamics tell you how that sensitivity should be transported backward to earlier times. The perturbations are not chosen as random samples or manually specified offsets. They are infinitesimal coordinate-direction questions: what would happen if x were slightly different, or y, or z, or v_x, and so on. Automatic differentiation and the adjoint equations compute the answers efficiently without explicitly perturbing each variable one by one.

A finite-difference version would be conceptually possible but inefficient. You could run the trajectory once, compute the loss, then nudge the initial x-position by a tiny amount, rerun the trajectory, and see how the loss changes. Then you could do the same for y, z, v_x, v_y, v_z, and eventually every neural network weight. That would give you numerical sensitivities, but it would be extremely expensive, especially because the neural network may have thousands or millions of parameters.

The adjoint method avoids this by computing the sensitivity of the loss to all relevant variables in one backward pass. It is analogous to backpropagation in a standard neural network. In a feedforward neural network, you do not manually perturb each weight to see how the loss changes. You run the network forward, compute the loss, and then backpropagation sends sensitivity backward through the layers. The adjoint method is the continuous-time analogue of that idea. Instead of backpropagating through discrete layers, you backpropagate through a differential equation solve.

For a standard neural network, the computation looks like a chain of layers: layer 1 produces layer 2, layer 2 produces layer 3, and eventually you get a loss. For a neural ODE, the computation is a continuous-time chain: the state at t_0 produces the state at t_1, which produces the state at t_2, and so on. The adjoint is the sensitivity signal moving backward through that time-continuous computation.

In your astrodynamics case, the forward pass says: starting from this initial position and velocity, the learned acceleration field produces this orbit. The loss says: this predicted orbit differs from the true 2BP orbit by this amount. The adjoint pass says: to reduce that orbit error, the model needed different state evolution at certain earlier places along the trajectory. Then the parameter-gradient calculation says: these are the neural network weights that would have changed the learned acceleration field in the useful directions.

So the adjoint is computed by differentiating through the relationship between the initial state, the learned dynamics, the predicted trajectory, and the loss. The “perturbation” language is interpretive. It means “if this variable had been infinitesimally different, how would the loss have changed?” It does not mean that you manually decide a perturbation and rerun the simulation.

For your full-orbit 2BP setup, the flow is: integrate the learned model forward over the orbit, compute position and velocity errors at saved time points, use those errors to define direct loss sensitivities, propagate those sensitivities backward through the predicted trajectory, and accumulate sensitivity with respect to the neural network parameters. The result is a gradient telling the optimizer how to update the weights.

So, non-mathematically: the loss identifies what was wrong, the adjoint traces that wrongness backward through the simulated orbit, and the gradient connects that backward-traced sensitivity to the neural network weights. No explicit perturbations are chosen. The perturbations are infinitesimal “what if this coordinate changed?” directions, and the adjoint method computes their effects efficiently.

There is no universal rule that the adjoint must monotonically increase, decay, or peak at periapsis. Its behavior depends on the loss, the trajectory, the learned model error, and the local dynamics. But there are some qualitative expectations.

Near periapsis, the dynamics are more sensitive to position because the gravitational field changes rapidly with radius. In 2BP, the acceleration magnitude scales like

\frac{1}{r^2},

and the gravity-gradient sensitivity scales roughly like

\frac{1}{r^3}.

That acceleration perturbation can then affect velocity, and the velocity error can affect the rest of the orbit. If your loss is accumulated over the full orbit, large adjoint values may appear at times that are most responsible for future trajectory mismatch. Sometimes that is near periapsis because the dynamics are highly sensitive there. Sometimes it is earlier than a visible error, because the adjoint is tracing the cause of later errors backward. Sometimes it is near places where the predicted and true trajectories have developed a large phase difference.

As the orbit continues, that velocity error produces a growing phase error. The loss may become large later in the orbit, but the adjoint can propagate that sensitivity backward and assign importance to the periapsis region, because that is where the learned acceleration error caused the downstream mismatch.

If you start exactly near periapsis and use a terminal loss after one period, I would expect the velocity adjoint to be important, because small early velocity differences can create accumulated phase error over the orbit. In the adjoint formulation, the gradient of the loss w.r.t. parameters is:

$$\frac{dL}{d\theta} = -\int_{t_1}^{t_0} \mathbf{a}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f(\mathbf{z}(t), t, \theta)}{\partial \theta} , dt$$

where:

- $\mathbf{z}(t)$ is the state trajectory (your $[\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{v}]$)
- $f(\mathbf{z}(t), t, \theta)$ is the vector field — what your MLP outputs
- $\mathbf{a}(t)$ is the **adjoint state**, a vector the same size as $\mathbf{z}$ that evolves backward in time according to its own ODE
- $\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$ is the Jacobian of the vector field w.r.t. all model parameters

The adjoint $\mathbf{a}(t)$ starts at $t_1$ (end of segment) initialized from $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{z}(t_1)}$ — how sensitive the loss is to the final state — and integrates **backward** to $t_0$. It accumulates how much each point along the trajectory contributed to the final loss.

**The connection to your batch loss:**

Your actual training gradient is this integral evaluated independently for each of the 64 segments in the batch, then averaged:

$$\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{dL^{(i)}}{d\theta} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} -\int_{t_0^{(i)}}^{t_1^{(i)}} \mathbf{a}^{(i)}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}(t), t, \theta)}{\partial \theta} , dt$$

**What this tells you about the apoapsis problem:**

The integrand $\mathbf{a}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$ is the gradient contribution at each moment in time. For a segment that passes through apoapsis:

- $\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$ is small there because the network outputs small accelerations and the input `1/r` is small — the MLP is operating in a low-sensitivity regime
- $\mathbf{a}(t)$ may also be small if the loss is insensitive to errors at that point along the trajectory

Both terms being small at apoapsis means the integrand is near zero there — the apoapsis region contributes almost nothing to $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta}$ even though the adjoint integrates through it. This is the mathematical statement of the weak gradient signal hypothesis, expressed directly in terms of the training gradient.

**On the family of orbits:**

This is the most important one to push back on. Training on 4 Lyapunov orbits at different amplitudes samples 4 curves in 4D phase space — together they cover a 2D surface (the orbit family manifold). The stable and unstable manifolds of each orbit are transverse to this family manifold. They live in the other 2 directions of the 4D space that the training data doesn't touch.

You can check this explicitly: compute the angle between the family continuation direction (the vector from one orbit's IC to the next orbit's IC in phase space) and the unstable eigenvector at the same point. If they're nearly orthogonal, the family gives essentially zero coverage in the unstable direction. If they overlap significantly, the family does some of the work. This is an empirical question worth computing before assuming the family is sufficient.

**On segmentation removing the IC notion:**

You're right, and this is actually more of a problem than it first appears. Segmentation turns the training into: given this state on the orbit, predict the next 20 steps. The model sees many states along the orbit as independent starting points. But — and this is the key — all of those starting states are on the orbit. In 4D phase space the model is still only seeing states on the 1D orbit curve. It has complete freedom to define whatever dynamics it wants in the 2 directions transverse to the orbit, and it picks the path of least resistance: smooth, neutral dynamics.

**On CR3BP sensitivity making off-orbit training hard:**

The sensitivity is only dangerous over long integration times. Over a very short segment — say 0.01T — a perturbation of ε in the unstable direction grows by 2600^0.01 ≈ 1.08. Barely anything. You could train on extremely short trajectories from perturbed ICs and the training signal would be useful without the trajectory escaping. The sensitivity is not a fundamental barrier to off-orbit training; it just requires short enough segments that the instability hasn't had time to act.

**But here's the deeper adversarial point that cuts across all three:**

This is a non-identifiability problem. With data only on the orbit curves, there are infinitely many vector fields consistent with the training data — any smooth function that agrees on those curves works. The model is finding one of those infinitely many solutions. It happens to be the wrong one because the optimizer prefers smooth, neutral dynamics. No amount of better optimization, more orbits in the family, or cleverer segmentation can fix non-identifiability. You need either more informative data (off-orbit trajectories) or additional constraints that rule out the wrong solutions.

The constraint you're willing to use: Jacobi conservation. This is physically meaningful, doesn't require knowing the acceleration directly, and rules out a large class of wrong solutions — specifically any dynamics that don't conserve energy. A model that conserves Jacobi can't have λ_u_model ≈ 1 for an orbit with λ_u_true ≈ 2600, because a Jacobi-conserving model with neutral stability would produce a family of quasi-periodic orbits, which is a qualitatively different phase space structure from the true saddle. Adding Jacobi drift as an auxiliary loss is the most principled thing you can do within your stated constraints.

**What to actually do next:**

Before any of that, run the single-orbit overfit test. Train on exactly 1 orbit with no curriculum, maximum steps, and log gradient norms explicitly. This tells you whether the issue is capacity, optimization, or data. If the model can't overfit 1 orbit with direct access to all gradient information, the problem is architectural and everything else is irrelevant.

What are floquet multipliers? What is a useful sort of perturbation analysis that we could do 

The monodromy matrix depends on which point on the orbit you choose as your starting point. If you start at point A and integrate for one full period back to A, you get M_A. If you start at point B (somewhere else on the same orbit) and integrate for one full period back to B, you get a different matrix M_B.

**But the eigenvalues are the same regardless of where you start.**

This is provable algebraically. If you start at B instead of A, the monodromy matrix transforms as:

```
M_B = Φ(B→A) · M_A · Φ(A→B)
```

where Φ(A→B) is the flow map from A to B. This is called a **similarity transformation**, and similarity transformations preserve eigenvalues. So λ_u ≈ 2600 is a property of the orbit itself, not of any particular starting point on it.

**The eigenvectors do change with the evaluation point.** The unstable direction at point A in phase space points in a different direction than the unstable direction at point B — the manifold curves as you move along the orbit. What stays constant is the growth rate, not the direction.

---

## What this means for our computation

We computed M at the training IC — the first point in the orbit states, which for Lyapunov orbits is typically the x-axis crossing. That's a natural and convenient choice.

The λ_u ≈ 2600 we got is a global property of each orbit and is correct regardless of where we evaluated it. The eigenvectors we extracted are the stable/unstable directions _at that specific point_, which is why we used them to perturb the IC and not some arbitrary other point on the orbit.

If we wanted to probe the model's accuracy in the stable/unstable directions at other points along the orbit, we'd need to propagate the eigenvectors forward using the state transition matrix — but for diagnosing the model we don't need that level of detail yet.

**Adversarial point on "instability blows up gradients":**

When you say instability blows up gradients, you're describing a symptom, not the cause. The actual mechanism is this: the gradient of the loss flows backward through the ODE solver via the adjoint equations. That backward propagation involves the state transition matrix Φ(T, 0) — which is the monodromy matrix. If λ_u ≈ 2600, then the condition number of Φ(T, 0) is roughly λ_u / λ_s ≈ 2600 / 0.0004 ≈ 6.5 million. A condition number that large means the gradient computation is numerically ill-conditioned — small errors in the loss get amplified enormously when you backpropagate through a full period.

So segmentation isn't just a hack to keep gradients small. It's a direct response to the instability that the Floquet multipliers describe. Shorter segments mean smaller Φ(τ, 0), smaller condition number, more stable gradient flow.

**But here's the adversarial version of that connection:**

Segmentation solves the gradient problem by preventing the optimizer from ever seeing the consequences of the instability. Over a segment of length 0.3T, the unstable growth is λ_u^0.3 ≈ 20x instead of 2600x. The loss gradient is tractable — but it's also informationally blind to the full instability. The model never receives a training signal that says "your predicted dynamics in the unstable direction is wrong by a factor of 2600." It only ever sees "your trajectory is off by a manageable amount after 0.3 periods."

So the same property that makes training hard (ill-conditioned gradients over full periods) also creates the failure mode (model doesn't learn the stability structure). You cannot fix both with the same workaround. Segmentation trades one problem for another.

---

**On computing monodromy at multiple orbit points:**

Here's where I want to push back on your intuition. You might be thinking "more evaluation points gives more information." But recall what we established: for the **true** dynamics, the Floquet multipliers are invariant around the orbit — λ_u ≈ 2600 everywhere. So computing M_true at 4 different phases gives you 4 identical sets of eigenvalues. Zero additional information about the orbit's stability.

**However** — and this is where it gets interesting — that invariance only holds for the true dynamics because those dynamics are actually consistent and the orbit is actually periodic. The model is not guaranteed to have either property. The model might produce a trajectory that _approximately_ closes after one period (hence low percent error at t=T) without having a true periodic orbit or internally consistent dynamics.

So for **M_model**, the eigenvalues are NOT constrained to be the same at different phases. If you compute M_model at four points around the orbit and get four different λ_u_model values, that's telling you the model doesn't even have a coherent notion of what the dynamics look like around the orbit. That's a different — and deeper — failure mode than consistently having λ_u_model ≈ 1.

Concretely, you might find:

- **λ_u_model ≈ 1 at all phases**: the model consistently thinks the orbit is stable — a uniform, systematic wrong belief
- **λ_u_model wildly varying (e.g., 0.5 to 50)**: the model has internally inconsistent dynamics — it's stitching together locally different vector fields that happen to produce an approximately closed trajectory when started from the training IC
- **λ_u_model increasing toward the correct value at the training IC phase and degrading elsewhere**: phase-anchoring in the stability structure, not just phase-anchoring in trajectory error

That third pattern would be the most revealing. It would confirm that the model learned _something_ about the instability near the training IC but failed to generalize it around the orbit — which is a much more specific diagnosis than just "wrong Floquet multipliers."

So yes, worth implementing — but the reason it's useful is not "more data points about the orbit's stability." It's "a consistency check on whether the model has coherent dynamics at all."






**The adjoint method and what decays:**

In the adjoint formulation, the gradient integrand is `a(t)^T ∂f/∂θ`. The adjoint state `a(t)` starts at `t1` (initialized from the loss gradient) and propagates _backward_ in time. The adjoint ODE is:

```
da/dt = -a(t)^T ∂f/∂z
```

The key is: the adjoint propagates backward through the same Jacobian structure that governs the forward instability. And here's the precise Floquet connection — the adjoint propagator over one period backward has eigenvalues that are the **reciprocals** of the Floquet multipliers.

So for our orbit:

- Forward unstable direction: λ_u ≈ 2600 — trajectories diverge
- Adjoint in that direction (going backward): 1/2600 ≈ 0.0004 — **the adjoint decays**

This means the gradient is vanishing specifically in the unstable direction. The signal about "your parameters controlling the unstable mode are wrong" decays by a factor of 0.0004 per period of backward propagation. By the time you reach early timesteps, that signal is effectively zero.

**But there's a second simultaneous problem you didn't mention:**

The stable direction has the reciprocal behavior. λ_s ≈ 0.0004 forward, so the adjoint grows by 1/0.0004 ≈ 2500 going backward in the stable direction. That's a potentially _exploding_ gradient. So you have:

- Vanishing gradient in the direction that matters most (unstable)
- Potentially exploding gradient in the other direction (stable)

Both come from the same large condition number of the backward propagator — roughly 2600/0.0004 ≈ 6.5 million. Segmentation reduces the condition number to λ_u^0.3 / λ_s^0.3 — still large but tractable.

**Now the adversarial point — and this is important:**

Your segmentation explanation assumes the problem is purely numerical: the gradients vanish, segmentation fixes that, and then the model can learn correctly. But the Floquet analysis reveals something more fundamental.

The gradient vanishes _specifically in the unstable direction_. That means even if you could somehow stabilize the adjoint computation over a full period, the gradient signal telling the model "your dynamics in the unstable direction are wrong" would still be small — because the adjoint is inherently suppressed in exactly that direction by the forward instability structure.

Think about what this means: the model receives essentially no gradient signal about its behavior in the unstable manifold direction, regardless of segment length. Segmentation helps stabilize the computation, but it doesn't create a gradient signal that was never there. The loss function — position error along the nominal trajectory — is simply not sensitive to errors in the unstable direction over any timescale short enough to have tractable gradients.

**So to directly answer your question:** Yes, there is a tight Floquet connection. The vanishing gradient and the trajectory instability are the same phenomenon, viewed from two sides. The number λ_u ≈ 2600 simultaneously causes trajectories to escape in one period _and_ causes the adjoint to lose sensitivity to early parameter errors in one period. Segmentation is a patch on the computational problem, not on the fundamental one: the training objective has no useful gradient signal about stability structure at any tractable training horizon.

**On computing monodromy at multiple orbit points:**

This is worth doing, but push back on why. The true Floquet multipliers are invariant — you'll get λ_u ≈ 2600 everywhere — so you learn nothing new about the orbit. But the model's eigenvalues are not constrained to be invariant. **CR3BP metric(s):**
Our neural ODE is learning to predict the CR3BP acceleration vector at any point in p-hase space. Given a state $[x, y, z, v_x, v_y, v_z]$ in the rotating frame, it ouputs $[a_x, a_y, a_z]$. $$
w_{\mathrm{region}}(\mathbf{x})
=
\sum_{i=1}^{5}
\alpha_i

\right),

$$

What is an invariant manifold? For a dynamical system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ with a flow map $\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)$, an invariant manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is a set of states that maps into itself under the dynamics $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathcal{M} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) \in \mathcal{M}$. In CR3BP trajectory design, these manifolds are usually associated with an equilibrium point or a periodic orbit like Lyapunov, halo, NRHO orbits. **In plain terms, an invariant manifold is a geometric set in phase space that the dynamics cannot leave.**

The manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is invariant if starting on it means the dynamics keeps you on it: $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathcal{M} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) \in \mathcal{M}.$

In words, if you start on the manifold you will stay there.

What is a flow map? A flow map is the oeprator that takes an initial state and returns the state reached after evolving under the dynamics for some time - basically it is representing propagation of an initial condition under specified dynamics. In words: $\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0) = \text{the point reached by propagating } \mathbf{x}_0 \text{ forward by time } t$, e.g. it is the exact nonlinear propagation operator for the dynamical system.

The STM is related but not the same object. The flow map is nonlinear in general $\mathbf{x}(t) = \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)$, while the STM is the local linearization of that flow map with respect to the initial condition $\mathbf{\Phi}(t,0) = \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}(t)}{\partial \mathbf{x}_0} = \frac{\partial \Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0)}{\partial \mathbf{x}_0}$. So, the STM is the Jacobian of the flow map with repsect to the initial state. The flow map tells you where a particular initial condition goes, but a STM tells you how a small perturbation to that initial condition evolves.

>

>

>

>

> [!note]- Monodromy matrix as it relates to stable and unstable manifolds
> For a periodic orbit, the stable and unstable manifolds are generated from the linearized dynamics around the orbit. You compute the monodromy matrix, which is the state transition matrix over one full period:
> 
> \mathbf{\Phi}(T,0).
> 
> Its eigenvalues and eigenvectors describe the local behavior near the periodic orbit. If the periodic orbit has one real eigenvalue outside the unit circle,
> 
> |\lambda_u| > 1,
> 
> then the corresponding eigenvector gives the local unstable direction. Perturbing the periodic orbit slightly along that direction and propagating forward produces the unstable manifold.
> 
> If it has one real eigenvalue inside the unit circle,
> 
> |\lambda_s| < 1,
> 
> then the corresponding eigenvector gives the local stable direction. Perturbing along that direction and propagating backward is a common way to trace out the stable manifold. Forward in time, those trajectories approach the periodic orbit.
> 
> A quasi-stable manifold is a less universal term. It is not as mathematically standard as stable or unstable manifold. In astrodynamics, people may use “quasi-stable” to describe trajectories or structures that remain near a region for a long time but are not truly asymptotically stable.
> 
> That distinction matters. A true stable manifold has an asymptotic property:
> 
> \operatorname{dist}\left(\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0), \gamma\right)
> \to 0
> \quad \text{as} \quad t \to +\infty.
> 
> A quasi-stable trajectory may only satisfy something weaker, such as:
> 
> \operatorname{dist}\left(\Phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0), \gamma\right)
> \text{ remains small for a long but finite time.}
> 
> So “quasi-stable” usually means “temporarily stable-looking” or “weakly escaping,” not truly stable in the invariant-manifold sense.

A **Hamiltonian system** describes dynamics in terms of an energy-like scalar function called the **Hamiltonian**:

$$
H(q,p)
$$

where $q$ represents generalized position coordinates and $p$ represents generalized momentum coordinates.

For many mechanical systems, the Hamiltonian is the total mechanical energy:

$$
H(q,p) = T(p) + V(q),
$$

so

$$
\text{Hamiltonian} = \text{kinetic energy} + \text{potential energy}.
$$

Instead of saying “forces cause accelerations,” Hamiltonian mechanics says that the **energy landscape determines how position and momentum evolve through phase space**.

The equations of motion are Hamilton’s equations:

$$
\dot{q} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p},
\qquad
\dot{p} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}.
$$

The first equation says that the way energy changes with momentum determines how position evolves. The second says that the way energy changes with position determines how momentum evolves, with a minus sign. That minus sign is what gives Hamiltonian motion its characteristic circulating behavior in phase space.

For a simple mass-spring system,

$$
H(q,p)
=
\frac{p^2}{2m}
+
\frac{1}{2}kq^2.
$$

The system trades energy between kinetic and potential energy. When the mass is far from equilibrium, energy is mostly potential. When it passes through equilibrium, energy is mostly kinetic. In phase space $(q,p)$, the trajectory forms closed loops because total energy is conserved.

So a useful intuition is that Hamiltonian motion follows contours of constant total energy in phase space

It does not usually move “downhill” like a dissipative system. Instead, it moves **around** the energy landscape. That is why Hamiltonian systems are strongly associated with conservative dynamics, oscillators, orbital motion, and phase-space structure.

For example, in orbital mechanics, the two-body problem can be written as a Hamiltonian system:

$$
H(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{p})
=
\frac{\|\mathbf{p}\|^2}{2m}
-
\frac{\mu m}{\|\mathbf{r}\|}.
$$

The first term is kinetic energy and the second term is gravitational potential energy. In the ideal two-body problem, the spacecraft does not spiral inward or outward because no energy is being added or removed. Its trajectory is constrained by the constant-energy structure of the system.

A useful contrast is a gradient system. A gradient system moves downhill on a scalar function:

$$
\dot{x} = -\nabla V(x).
$$

A Hamiltonian system instead moves perpendicular, in a structured sense, to the gradient of the Hamiltonian:

$$
\dot{x} = J \nabla H(x),
$$

where

$$
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
q \\
p
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
J =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & I \\
-I & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The matrix $J$ rotates the energy gradient into the direction of motion. That is why the system circulates around energy contours instead of descending them.

Intuitively, a Hamiltonian system is a conservative dynamical system where the total-energy function defines the geometry of motion. The state evolves through phase space in a way that preserves energy and follows structured position-momentum coupling.
