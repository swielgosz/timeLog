**latent activation vs final activation vs drift output activation:**
**`latent_activation`** The hidden-layer activation used in all MLP subnetworks — the encoder, decoder, and drift network. Applied between every intermediate layer. Default: `tanh`.

**`latent_final_activation`** The activation applied at the _output layer_ of the encoder and drift MLPs (but **not** the decoder — the decoder's final activation is hardcoded to identity so it can produce unbounded reconstructions). Default: `tanh`.

**`latent_drift_output_activation`** An _additional_ activation applied to the drift MLP's output _after_ it has already passed through `latent_final_activation`, before being scaled by `latent_drift_scale`. This is separate from the MLP's own final layer — it's applied at the `LatentDynamics.__call__` level:

```python
nonlinear_term = self.output_scale * self.output_activation(self.mlp(z))
```

So it gates the magnitude of the learned dynamics vector field. Default: `tanh`.

In practice, since all three default to `tanh`, the most impactful one to tune is `latent_drift_output_activation` — switching it to `linear` (identity) removes the output saturation on the drift, which can help if the dynamics are being damped too aggressively. `latent_final_activation` affects how the encoder compresses into the initial latent state, which matters for the shape of the latent manifold.

## What is drift scale?
`nonlinear_term = self.output_scale * self.output_activation(self.mlp(z))`
where `output_scale` is initialized to `drift_scale.` The full latent vector field is:
`dz/dt = drift_scale * tanh(MLP(z))` (does Patrick Kidger do this?)
It is a scalar multiplier on the output of the dynamics MLP which boiunds how fast `z` can change per unit time. Since tanh is bounded in `[-1, 1]`, the maximum rate of change in any latent dimension is drift_scale. With drift_scale = `0.02, |dz_i/dt| ≤ 0.02` always.

Whether that's too small depends entirely on the scale of your normalized time and how much z actually needs to move over one orbit. If a typical orbit spans t ∈ [0, 100] in normalized units, then z can travel at most 0.02 × 100 = 2.0 units — which might be fine. If the orbit spans t ∈ [0, 10], it can only move 0.2 total, which would be very restrictive.

So the real question is: what is the typical time span of your training segments in normalized units, and how much does z need to move to represent a full orbit? You can check this by logging |z(t_end) - z(t_0)| during training.

There may be competing forces . The drift scale does increase from 0.02 to about 0.1 - the latent_dift_norm_weight penalizes large drift normas, which caps how much output_scale can grow




The most informative next diagnostic would be to also log `|z(t_end) - z(t_start)|` (the actual latent displacement over a training segment) to see if the latent trajectory is actually moving meaningfully or staying nearly flat despite `output_scale` reaching 0.1.

This is exactly what your **latent closure metric** already computes (`latent_closure_rel_l2` in the diagnostics). If that ratio is small (say < 0.1), the latent trajectory is barely moving — the ODE is nearly static and the model is essentially doing encode-once-decode-everywhere. If it's large (> 1), the trajectory is moving substantially relative to its own scale.

But even that doesn't tell you if the motion is _correct_ — just that it's happening. The more meaningful question is whether `z(t)` is tracing a coherent path that the decoder can follow. The rollout failure you're seeing suggests one of two things:

1. **z(t) moves but the decoder can't follow it** — the latent trajectory drifts into regions of latent space the decoder wasn't trained on, producing garbage outputs. This would show up as good `latent_closure_rel` but bad decoded rollout.
    
2. **z(t) barely moves** — the ODE is nearly static, so the decoded output is nearly constant (just the initial decoded point repeated). This would show up as very small `latent_closure_rel`.
    

You're already logging `latent_closure_rel_l2` — what value is it at during training? That would immediately tell you which regime you're in.

Why would differing time scales matter for latent ODE but not vanilla neural ODE? 
In a **vanilla neural ODE**, the vector field operates directly in physical state space `(x, y, z, vx, vy, vz)`. The physics naturally encodes the period information — a satellite at radius r with speed v implicitly tells the ODE "you're moving fast/slow" because the state itself carries that information. The network learns `dy/dt = f(y)` where `y` contains everything needed to determine the rate of change.

In the **latent ODE**, the vector field operates on `z` — an 8-dimensional abstract representation that has no guaranteed physical meaning. When you encode a fast LEO orbit and a slow GEO orbit, they might map to similar regions of latent space (similar shape, different scale), but the ODE needs to move them at completely different rates. The vector field `dz/dt = f(z)` has no way to know which orbit it's integrating unless the period information is somehow encoded into z itself.

So the problem isn't time normalization per se — it's that **the latent code z may not contain enough information to determine the correct integration speed**. In physical space this is automatic; in latent space it has to be learned.

This is actually an argument for the **auxiliary orbital head** you already have — supervising z0 to predict `[a, e, E, sin(ν), cos(ν)]` should force period-relevant information (semi-major axis determines period via Kepler's third law) into the latent code, giving the ODE something to work with. But with `aux_orbital_weight: 1e-2` it may be too weak to actually enforce this structure.

### Low thrust
As written,
$\mathbf a_{\text{unk}} = a_0 \cos(\nu)\,\hat{\mathbf t}$ 
is best understood as a synthetic periodic perturbation, not a realistic low-thrust law that would naturally come from an actual spacecraft thruster. The main reason is that real low-thrust propulsion is usually governed by actuator commands, available power, pointing constraints, duty cycle, and mission objectives. It is not typically a smooth sinusoid in true anomaly with sign reversals every half orbit.

There are two separate questions here: is it physically possible, and is it operationally realistic? Physically, yes, you could imagine a controller commanding an along-track thrust that varies smoothly with orbital phase. In that sense it is not nonsense. But operationally, it is not how a real EP system would usually be modeled unless you explicitly posit a feedback or open-loop guidance law of the form
$T(\nu) = T_0 \cos(\nu)$.
Without that additional story, it looks artificial.

The biggest realism issue is the sign change. When \cos \nu < 0, the acceleration reverses and points opposite the velocity direction. That means the spacecraft is alternately accelerating and braking each orbit. A thruster can certainly do that if you rotate the thrust direction or command reverse along-track acceleration through attitude control, but that is no longer a simple “continuous low-thrust in the velocity direction” model. It is a deliberately phase-modulated control law.

So I would classify it this way. As a benchmark unknown perturbation for Neural ODE learning, it is very good. As a realistic propulsion model, it is weak unless you explicitly say it represents a commanded periodic control profile.

If you want something more realistic while keeping it smooth and learnable, these are better options.

The most realistic simple model is always-on low thrust with small periodic modulation:
$\mathbf a_{\text{unk}}=a_0\left(1+\epsilon \cos \nu\right)\hat{\mathbf t},\qquad 0<\epsilon<1.$ 
This is much more defensible. The thrust stays mostly along-track, does not reverse sign, and varies smoothly with orbital phase. That could plausibly represent throttle variation due to power variation, imperfect control, or a deliberately scheduled guidance profile.

If you want a purely periodic perturbation with zero mean, that is mathematically clean but less realistic as propulsion:
$\mathbf a_{\text{unk}} = a_1 \cos(\nu)\,\hat{\mathbf t}$.
That is fine for a system identification test, but I would describe it as a synthetic periodic along-track forcing, not “realistic low thrust.”

Another more realistic route is to make the modulation depend on Sun geometry or eclipse rather than directly on true anomaly. For example, a solar-electric thruster could have reduced available power in certain orbital phases. A smooth surrogate would be
$\mathbf a_{\text{unk}} =a_0\left(1+\epsilon \cos \phi\right)\hat{\mathbf t}$,
where $\phi$ is an orbital phase relative to the Sun line, not just true anomaly. That has a clearer physical interpretation.

For your goal, the best compromise is probably:
$\mathbf a_{\text{unk}}=a_0\left(1+0.2\cos\nu\right)\hat{\mathbf t}$.

Why this is better:
the acceleration remains positive along-track, so it looks like a continuously firing thruster rather than a thrust/brake oscillation; it is still smooth and periodic; and it is still easy for a Neural ODE to learn because it is low-frequency and autonomous when written in terms of state.

If you want something even cleaner from a learning perspective, you can also use argument of latitude u instead of true anomaly for near-circular cases, but for generic 2BP work$ $\nu$ is fine.

So the precise answer is: $a_0\cos(\nu)\hat{\mathbf t}$ is not very realistic as a standalone propulsion model, but it is perfectly reasonable as a synthetic, smooth, periodic unknown perturbation. If you want a more realistic “low-thrust-like” perturbation, use a positive baseline plus small harmonic modulation instead of a zero-mean sign-changing sinusoid.

I would write it in a paper as either

$\mathbf a_{\text{unk}} = a_1\cos(\nu)\,\hat{\mathbf t}$
and call it a synthetic periodic along-track perturbation,

or

$\mathbf a_{\text{unk}} = a_0(1+\epsilon\cos\nu)\,\hat{\mathbf t}$
and call it a phase-modulated continuous low-thrust acceleration.

The second description is much easier to defend as “realistic enough.”## SRP

### SRP
The form I wrote,

$$
\mathbf a_{\mathrm{SRP}} \approx -a_{\mathrm{SRP},0},\hat{\mathbf s},
$$

is the same model written in compressed notation, with the constant

$$
a_{\mathrm{SRP},0} = P_{\mathrm{SRP}},C_R,\frac{A}{m}
$$

if you neglect the Sun-distance variation, or more generally

$$
a_{\mathrm{SRP},0}(R) = P_{\mathrm{SRP}}(R),C_R,\frac{A}{m}
$$

with

$$
P_{\mathrm{SRP}}(R)=4.57\times 10^{-6}\left(\frac{R_0}{R}\right)^2 \ \mathrm{N/m^2}.
$$

That is exactly the expression given in the NASA paper for the cannonball model.

The only thing to watch is the sign convention. The NASA paper defines $\mathbf r_s$ as the normalized Sun-to-spacecraft direction and writes

$$
\mathbf a_{\mathrm{SRP}} = P_{\mathrm{SRP}} C_R \frac{A}{m_{\mathrm{sat}}},\mathbf r_s.
$$

So the acceleration points away from the Sun. If instead you define $\hat{\mathbf s}$ as the spacecraft-to-Sun direction, then you need a minus sign:

$$
\mathbf a_{\mathrm{SRP}} = -P_{\mathrm{SRP}} C_R \frac{A}{m_{\mathrm{sat}}},\hat{\mathbf s}.
$$

Those are equivalent; it is just a direction-definition issue.

So the correspondence is:

$$
\mathbf r_s \equiv \text{unit vector from Sun to spacecraft},
$$

$$
\hat{\mathbf s} \equiv \text{unit vector from spacecraft to Sun} = -\mathbf r_s.
$$

Then

$$
P_{\mathrm{SRP}} C_R \frac{A}{m},\mathbf r_s
	•	P_{\mathrm{SRP}} C_R \frac{A}{m},\hat{\mathbf s}.
$$

Also, the paper’s statement that $C_R\in[1,2]$ for the simple cannonball model is consistent with the usual absorbed-to-reflected interpretation in that simplified setting. In this model, the acceleration magnitude is proportional to the area-to-mass ratio:

$$
\left|\mathbf a_{\mathrm{SRP}}\right| = P_{\mathrm{SRP}} C_R \frac{A}{m}.
$$

$$
\mathbf a_{\mathrm{unk}} = a_0(1+\epsilon\cos\nu),\hat{\mathbf t},
$$

but if your learned augmentation only takes spacecraft state $\mathbf x$ and not Sun geometry or time/phase, then SRP is generally not autonomous from the model’s perspective. That is the main caveat, not the force expression.

## CR3BP Phase Space Coverage Metrics

All metrics are grounded in the **Jacobi constant** C_J, which is conserved along every CR3BP trajectory:

$$C_J = 2\Omega(x,y,z) - v^2$$

At each position, the accessible velocity set is a sphere of radius $v_{max} = \sqrt{2\Omega - C_J}$, and the accessible position space (Hill region) is where $2\Omega > C_J$.

---

### 1. Spatial (3D) Coverage

Divides the Hill region into a 3D voxel grid and counts the fraction of accessible voxels visited by at least one trajectory state, regardless of velocity direction. Fast to compute and easy to visualize, but it **overestimates** true phase space coverage: two trajectories passing through the same position at different velocities (e.g., stable vs. unstable manifold branches) both mark the same voxel as covered. Best used as a quick sanity check.

### 2. Manifold (5D) Coverage

The theoretically correct metric. The full energy manifold $M_{C_J}$ is a 5D space — 3 position coordinates plus velocity direction on $S^2$. Discretizes this into $(n_{xyz}^3 \times n_\theta \times n_\phi)$ cells and weights coverage by the manifold measure $v_{max}^2 \sin\theta$, so positions with larger speed budgets count more toward the total. Correctly distinguishes trajectories at the same position but different velocity directions.

### 3. Poincaré Section (4D) Coverage

Reduces the 5D manifold to the surface of section $y=0, \dot{y}>0$. At each crossing, the state is a 4D point $(x, z, \dot{x}, \dot{z})$ — velocity magnitude is fixed by $C_J$, so the accessible velocity region is a disk rather than a sphere. Best balance of rigor and interpretability: the section boundary is analytically clean, and the metric is directly comparable between training data and model-decoded trajectories. Implemented in `poincare_coverage.py`.

### 4. Monte Carlo Nearest-Neighbor (5D)

Randomly samples points from the 5D energy manifold and finds the distance from each sample to the nearest trajectory state in normalized 5D space (position / $r_{char}$, unit velocity direction). Coverage fraction = fraction of samples within distance $\varepsilon$; mean nearest-neighbor distance is a complementary density-sensitive scalar. Grid-free and avoids discretization artifacts, but sensitive to the choice of $r_{char}$ and $\varepsilon$.

---

### How they compare

|Metric|Dim|Speed|What it measures|
|---|---|---|---|
|Spatial|3D|Fast|Position diversity; ignores velocity direction|
|Manifold|5D|Medium|Full phase space including velocity direction|
|Poincaré|4D|Medium|Phase space at a canonical section; most interpretable|
|MC NN|5D|Medium|Density-sensitive coverage; no grid artifacts|

For the Earth-Moon CR3BP, absolute coverage values are typically low because orbit families occupy narrow dynamical corridors within the large accessible Hill region. The metrics are most useful **relatively** — comparing training data vs. model reconstructions, or comparing dataset diversity across different family compositions.

## Coverage metric ideas
Yes, you can measure coverage over the entire CR3BP phase space, but you must first decide what manifold you mean by “phase space,” because the Jacobi integral constrains the motion. For a fixed Jacobi constant C_J, the CR3BP dynamics lie on a **5-dimensional energy manifold** rather than the full 6-dimensional state space. The manifold is

$$

\mathcal{M}_{C_J} = {(x,y,z,\dot x,\dot y,\dot z)\mid C_J = 2\Omega(x,y,z) - (\dot x^2+\dot y^2+\dot z^2)}.

$$

Therefore any physically meaningful coverage metric should measure occupancy of this **5D energy manifold**, not the full state space.

The Jacobi constant also gives an analytic constraint on the admissible velocities. Rearranging the Jacobi integral gives

$$

\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + \dot z^2 = 2\Omega(x,y,z) - C_J.

$$

At each spatial point (x,y,z), the admissible velocity vectors lie on a sphere with radius

$$

v_{\max}(x,y,z) = \sqrt{2\Omega(x,y,z) - C_J}.

$$

Thus the accessible spatial region is defined by the **zero-velocity surface condition**

$$

2\Omega(x,y,z) > C_J,

$$

and at every allowed spatial point there is an attached velocity sphere.

  

This gives a clean analytic expression for the total accessible phase volume of the energy manifold. Integrating the velocity sphere volume over all spatial locations gives

$$

V_{\text{accessible}}(C_J) =

\iiint_{2\Omega(x,y,z) > C_J}

\frac{4\pi}{3}

\left(2\Omega(x,y,z) - C_J\right)^{3/2}

,dx,dy,dz.

$$

This expression is the direct analogue of the velocity-disk formula used for a Poincaré section, except now the admissible velocity region is a sphere rather than a disk.

  
**A global coverage metric can then be defined by discretizing the admissible region of the energy manifold and computing the fraction of cells that are actually visited by trajectories:**
 **$$\text{coverage}(C_J)=

\frac{N_{\text{visited}}}{N_{\text{accessible}}}.
$$

Each trajectory sample corresponds to a state $(x,y,z,\dot x,\dot y,\dot z)$ which is assigned to a phase-space cell; the numerator counts the number of visited cells, while the denominator counts all cells that satisfy the Jacobi constraint. In practice, the computational pipeline looks like this. First discretize spatial space over a region that includes the Hill region of interest, for example

$$

x \in [-2,2],\qquad y \in [-2,2],\qquad z \in [-1,1].

$$

At each spatial grid point evaluate the zero-velocity condition $2\Omega(x,y,z) > C_J$; points that fail this test are dynamically forbidden. For the remaining spatial cells compute the velocity radius $v_{\max}=\sqrt{2\Omega-C_J}$. Velocity space can then be discretized locally inside the sphere $\dot x^2+\dot y^2+\dot z^2 \le v_{\max}^2$. Trajectory states are mapped to these spatial-velocity cells and marked as visited, allowing the global coverage ratio to be computed.

  

However, a naive grid in five or six dimensions becomes computationally infeasible. For example, a 50^6 grid would require roughly $1.6\times10^{10}$ cells. Because of this, practical implementations typically use alternative approaches. One option is **Monte-Carlo sampling of the energy manifold**: randomly sample spatial points inside the Hill region, sample velocities uniformly inside the admissible sphere, and then estimate coverage statistically by checking whether nearby trajectory states exist. Another approach is **kernel density estimation**, where a density $p(x,y,z,\dot x,\dot y,\dot z)$ is estimated from the dataset and coverage is defined as the fraction of the manifold where this density exceeds some threshold. A third practical strategy is to measure coverage in lower-dimensional projections such as spatial coverage $(x,y,z)$, velocity coverage $(\dot x,\dot y,\dot z)$, and energy coverage $C_J$; these projections are much easier to interpret and often reveal deficiencies in the training dataset more clearly than a single global metric.

In astrodynamics literature, full energy-manifold coverage metrics are rarely used explicitly because of the dimensionality of the system. Instead, researchers typically rely on Poincaré maps, invariant manifold visualizations, and Jacobi-energy distributions to characterize the explored region of phase space. For machine-learning models of dynamics—such as neural ODE models of the CR3BP—the most informative evaluation usually combines several measures: spatial coverage of the Hill region, distribution of Jacobi constants, and coverage of Poincaré sections capturing invariant structures. Together these approximate the effective coverage of the energy manifold without requiring a full discretization of the 5-dimensional phase space.


## The Problem with 3D

For **planar** CR3BP, the Poincaré section `y=0, ẏ>0` collapses to a 2D map in `(x, ẋ)`. The accessible region at a given C_J is a bounded 2D area (inside the zero-velocity curve), so filling fraction is well-defined.

For **3D** orbits, the same section is now **4D**: `(x, z, ẋ, ż)`. The accessible region is a 4D volume bounded by the zero-velocity surface (itself 2D in position space). This means:

- A single 2D filling fraction **loses information** about the out-of-plane dimensions
- The normalization (total accessible area) is no longer trivial to compute
- Projecting to `(x, ẋ)` still works visually but conflates orbits with very different `z_max` — a halo orbit and a Lyapunov at the same C_J look similar in this projection

So options 2 and 3 are **partially meaningful** in projection, but you lose the clean "fraction of accessible region" interpretation.

---

## Better Alternatives for 3D Multi-Family Data

The natural replacement is to use the additional degree of freedom — out-of-plane excursion — as a second coverage axis:

**Primary metric:** C_J distribution (unchanged, always valid)

**Replace Poincaré filling fraction with:**

|Metric|What it captures|
|---|---|
|**C_J × z_max grid coverage**|Which energy levels AND out-of-plane amplitudes are covered — separates Lyapunov (z=0) from halo from vertical Lyapunov|
|**Orbit family × C_J bin count**|Since you have labeled families, directly measure family-energy coverage; cleanest interpretation|
|**2D Poincaré projection** `(x, ẋ)`|Still useful for visualizing in-plane structure, but drop the filling fraction normalization|

The `C_J × z_max` 2D histogram is probably your best single coverage metric — it captures the two conserved/quasi-conserved quantities that distinguish CR3BP orbit families in 3D, without requiring a 4D grid or a clean accessible-region boundary.

are z_max that distinct per family?

Honestly, not as much as I implied. Within a single family, z_max varies **continuously** as a parameter — it's not a label, it's a coordinate along the family. So in the `(C_J, z_max)` plane, each family traces out a **curve**, not a distinct region. Those curves can intersect.

Concretely for Earth-Moon:

- **Lyapunov**: z_max = 0 always — this one is cleanly separated
- **Halo**: z_max increases continuously from ~0 (at bifurcation from Lyapunov) up to large values (NRHOs). Small halos overlap with vertical Lyapunov in z_max
- **Vertical Lyapunov**: also large z_max, overlaps with halo in parts of (C_J, z_max) space
- **DRO**: nearly planar, small z_max — but there's a 3D DRO branch that extends it
- **Butterfly**: large z_max, near L2

So the `(C_J, z_max)` grid would separate Lyapunov from the 3D families cleanly, but not much else.

---

## What This Means for the Coverage Metric

If you have actual family labels in your dataset (which your code suggests you do, given `build_family_label_array`), the **orbit family × C_J bin count** is the more honest and directly interpretable metric — it uses the ground truth classification rather than a proxy that only partially discriminates. The `(C_J, z_max)` idea is only useful if labels aren't available or if you're evaluating _generated_ trajectories from the model that aren't pre-labeled.

### Most important training factors
When training a Latent ODE for 2BP and eventually CR3BP, the most important architectural decision is the latent dimension. It determines the intrinsic dimensionality of the learned dynamical system $dot{z} = f_\theta(z)$.

If the latent space is too small, the invariant manifold is distorted. If it is too large, you introduce artificial neutral directions and unstable Jacobian structure. For planar 2BP, use d_z = 4. For full 3D 2BP or CR3BP, start with d_z = 6. Only increase beyond that if you have clear evidence the model cannot represent the geometry. Avoid unnecessarily large latent spaces if you plan to analyze eigenstructure or perform continuation.

The ODE network should be shallow but moderately wide. Gravitational dynamics are smooth, not compositional, so depth is less important than width. Two hidden layers are usually sufficient. For planar 2BP, width 32–64 is typically adequate. For CR3BP, width 64–128 is reasonable. Increasing depth often worsens conditioning in Neural ODE training because gradients propagate through both time integration and layer composition. If performance is insufficient, increase width before increasing depth.

The encoder and decoder should be expressive enough to map between coordinate systems but not so powerful that they compensate for poor latent dynamics. Hidden sizes modestly larger than the latent dimension (e.g., 32–64 for d_z=4, 64–128 for d_z=6) are sufficient. If the decoder is too strong, it can mask dynamical defects, which is problematic if you care about stability, eigenvalues, or continuation.

Use smooth activations such as tanh or softplus. Non-smooth activations distort the Jacobian and degrade geometric fidelity. If you observe large spectral radii, excessive rejected solver steps, or unstable eigenstructure unrelated to physics, the ODE network is likely over-expressive.

In order of importance: choose the latent dimension carefully, then set ODE width, keep depth minimal, and avoid overly powerful decoders. For continuation and manifold analysis in CR3BP, minimal latent dimension and controlled expressivity are more important than raw reconstruction accuracy.

Learning rate will likely behave differently than the neuralODE and we may need to tune this further. The latent ODE has encoder, latent dynamics, decoder, and KL term so optimization is more coupled and may be more LR-snesitive. NeuralODE is impler in objective structure and often tolerates a cleaner LR regime. Posterior/inference effects in latent modles can make the same LR feel too aggressive or too weak depending on KL weight and segment length.
### Speeding up Training
Lower solver burden - looser tolerancces, cap `max_steps`, shorten early segment lengths (but not too much) if we are using a segmentation curriculum. Smaller architecture - smaller `hidden_size`, `width_size`, `latent_size`, `batch_size`. Use training curriculum more aggressively. Evaluate metrics less frequently. Pretrain on short segments then fine-tune on full length. 

### KL
The KL term is the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the approximate posterior over the latent initial condition and a chose prior. It measures how far your learned latent distribution deviates from a Gaussian prior. Its function: prevents the encoder from arbitrarily spreading or collapsing latent space; keeps latent trajectories in a controlled region near the prior; enforces smoothness and interpolation structure in latent space. 

the KL weight determines the trade-off between reconstruction accuracy and latent regularization.

## Latent ODE
```
(t, y(t))
  ↓
Encoder (GRU)
  ↓
(mean, log-std)
  ↓
Reparameterize → z₀
  ↓
latent_to_hidden(z₀) → h₀
  ↓
Latent ODE: dh/dt = fθ(h)
  ↓
Hidden trajectory h(t)
  ↓
Decoder (hidden_to_data)
  ↓
ŷ(t)
```

### Encoder
It reads the entire trajectory and compresses it into a single summary vector. It learns "what kind of orbit is this?" and encodes shape, size/energy, family membership, etc.

The encoder’s job is to compress an entire trajectory (t,y(t))(t,y(t)) into a single latent representation that seeds the ODE. It does this with a GRU that reads one trajectory at a time (in reverse order in this code) and produces a final hidden state h that summarizes the orbit. A linear layer maps h into two vectors, mean and logstd, which define a Gaussian distribution over latent initial conditions. We then sample z0z0​ with the reparameterization trick z0=mean+ϵ⋅stdz0​=mean+ϵ⋅std, where ϵ∼N(0,I)ϵ∼N(0,I). This keeps sampling differentiable so gradients can train the encoder.

That sampled z0z0​ is mapped to the ODE’s initial hidden state, integrated forward, and decoded back to y^(t)y^​(t). The encoder is trained every step alongside the latent dynamics and decoder, using a loss that combines reconstruction error with a KL term that nudges the latent distribution toward a standard normal. The key intuition is that the GRU is a trajectory summarizer, and the latent ODE learns to regenerate the full trajectory from that summary alone.

### `latent_to_hidden` and 'hidden_to_latent'
They’re opposites in the pipeline:
- hidden_to_latent is **encoder-side**. It takes the GRU’s hidden summary of a trajectory (h) and produces the parameters of the latent distribution (mean, logstd), which we sample z0 from.
- latent_to_hidden is **decoder/ODE-side**. It takes that sampled z0 and maps it into the ODE’s hidden state space (h0), which is then integrated over time.
So: hidden_to_latent maps **hidden → latent distribution**, while latent_to_hidden maps **latent code → ODE hidden state**.
latent_to_hidden is the small MLP that **maps the sampled latent code z0 into the ODE’s hidden state** h0. In other words, it converts from latent space (latent_size) to the ODE state space (hidden_size) so the ODE can be integrated.

`latent_to_hidden` turns "which orbit" into "where do I start on the hidden trajectory?" -- chooses the starting point of the learned dynamics. 

Hidden trajectory h(t) is the internal representation of motion, e.g. the time-evolving hidden latent state after integrating the latent ODE. This is like the orbit traced in a nice coordinate system.

`hidden_to_data` maps hidden states back to physical coordinates

`latent_size` = how many coordinates you use to label which orbit you're generating -- which orbit is this? (family identity). This is the dimension of the  *orbit-identity space*
`hidden_size` = how many coordinates the learned ODE uses to draw that orbit over time -- how do I trace this orbit in time? (time evolution). This is the dimension of the *learned dynamical state*

KL divergence (Kullback–Leibler divergence) measures how different two probability distributions are. In a VAE/latent ODE, it compares the encoder’s latent distribution q(z∣x)q(z∣x) to a prior (usually standard normal $p(z)=N(0,I)p(z)=N(0,I)$). KL = 0 means the two distributions are identical, while larger KL means the latent distribution is drifting away from the prior.
In training, the KL term regularizes the latent space so it stays “well‑behaved” instead of overfitting each example with a wildly different latent distribution.

## Jacobian Review
Jacobians are a local linear approximation of a nonlinear function. If we change the input a little, how does the ouput change?
If

$$f(y) =
\begin{bmatrix}
f_1(y_1, \dots, y_n) \\
f_2(y_1, \dots, y_n) \\
\vdots \\
f_m(y_1, \dots, y_n)
\end{bmatrix}$$

then the Jacobian is the matrix of partial derivatives:
$$J(y) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y_n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\frac{\partial f_m}{\partial y_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial y_n}
\end{bmatrix}$$
In our case, we have $\dot{y}=f_\theta(t,y)$
The Jacobian $J = \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial y}$

tells us about:
- stability
	- large negative real eigenvalues = fast decay (stiff modes)
	- positive real eigenvalues = local exponential growth (instability)
	- real parts near zero = slow modes
	- 
- stiffness -- if there is big separation in eigenvalue magnitudes:
	- solver struggles
	- gradients vanish/explode 
	- adjoint pain
- solver behavior
	- large eigenvalues $\rightarrow$ tiny step sizes
	- highly curved vector fields $\rightarrow$ lots of rejected steps
	- Jacobian spectrum can tell about numerical difficulty

How would we know if our vector field is highly curved from the Jacobian?
- Large entries in J $\rightarrow$ the vector field changes rapidly with position
- Large eigenvalues / large operator norm $\rightarrow$ strong local stretching / contraction
- Strong off-diagonal terms $\rightarrow$ shear and rotation
- Highly non-normal J $\rightarrow$ transient growth even if eigenvalues look “stable”
- Overall, big Jacobian means sharp bends, tight squeezing (big meaning big norm)

Terms to look up:
- frobenius norm
- spectral norm

Diagnostics:
- If jacobian norm is large, we have sharp local geometry
```
norm_J = jnp.linalg.norm(jac, ord=2)   # spectral norm approx
```
- does Jacobian vary a lot over the trajectory? If os, the vector field is changing rapidly
```
curvature_proxy = jnp.linalg.norm(jac_t1 - jac_t0) / (t1 - t0)
```
- If the eigenvalues jump around spatially, then we me have stiff and curved geomtry
``` 
spread = max(|Re(λ)|) / (min(|Re(λ)|) + eps)
```
Heuristic meaning of $\vert\vert J \vert\vert$ 
- ~O(1) are benign dynamics
- 10-100 is sharp dynamics, solver starts working hard
- 100-1e4 is stiff/pathological geometry
- >> 1e4 is solver compensation regime

## The Vanishing Gradient Problem for Stiff Neural Differential Equations
## Opt-disc vs disc-opt
Diffrax uses a discretize then optimize method. It differentiates through the discretized solver's operations rather than deriving continuous adjoint ODEs. The default for `diffeqsolve` in Diffrax is `RecursiveCheckpointAdjoint` which means the gradients are computed by differentiating the solver's computation graph (the discrete steps) rather than solving a continuous adjoint system. The original Neural ODE paper uses an optimize-then-discretize approach, the continuous adjoint method. In this method, derive the continuous adjoint equations then discretize them for numerical solution. - Diffrax opts instead to rely on autodiff through the solver’s time-stepping, which discretizes first and then differentiates that discrete computation. 
https://docs.kidger.site/diffrax/api/adjoints/

Stifness in neuralODEs usually comes from the network, not the physics. 

Standard explicit integrators like Runga-Kutta (what we use) are impractical for stiff problems. Explicit integrators compute the next step directly from known information, while implicit integrators define the next state implicitly and it must be solved for at each set. Take $\dot{x}(t) = f(x(t), t)$. 
Explicit step (forward Euler):
$x_{n+1} = x_n + h f(x_n, t_n)$
- Uses only x_n
- One function evaluation
- No equations to solve
Implicit step (Backward Euler):
$x_{n+1} = x_n + h f(x_{n+1}, t_{n+1})$
- $x_{n+1}$ appears on both sides
- Requires solving a nonlinear equation
- Often done with Newton iterations + Jacobians
- Stable but expensive - this is why we don't use them for neural ODEs typically

Implicit methods are the standard for integrating stiff ODEs. Recall that neural ODEs can become stiff throughout the training process, even if the original data comes from non-stiff ODEs, due to the nonlinear dynamics produced by the neural network. 

A- and L-stable integration methods. 

How can we tell if our neural ODE is stiff?
- Solver behavior. During training, log the number of function evaluations, average step size, and rejected steps if available. Warning if NFE steadily increases over epochs or spikes late in training, or if the solver takes much smaller steps than expected for smooth physics. 
- Compare explicit and implicit solvers - if performance improves with an implicit solver, stiffness is probably present

| **Observation**                           | **Interpretation**                  |
| ----------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Implicit solver takes much larger steps   | Learned vector field is stiff       |
| Explicit solver NFE ≫ implicit            | Stiffness confirmed                 |
| Implicit gradients are larger / healthier | Gradient damping was solver-induced |

- Jacobian spectrum check
	At sampled states x(t), compute:
	$J = \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial x}$ 
	Then look at:
		•	spectral radius
		•	real parts of eigenvalues
	Heuristic
		•	Large negative real eigenvalues (≫ 1 / timestep scale) → stiffness
		•	Rapid variation of eigenvalues along trajectory → stiffness
	Don't need to do this everywhere. Spot checks help. 
- Sensitivity decay
	Check:
	- adjoint norm as a function of time
	- gradient magnitude w.r.t. early-time states
	Classic stiff-Neural-ODE signature:
	- gradients near final time are OK
	- gradients near initial time are ~0
	- worsens as training progresses
	This happens even if loss decreases.
- Perturbation test
	Take a trained model, slightly perturb the state $x \to x + \epsilon$ and integrate forward. Dynamics are stiff if trajectories diverge violently, solver shrinks timestep immediately, or tiny perturbations cause large accelerations. 

Suggestions to help mitigate this:
- Regularize the Jacobian
	- spectral norm penalty
	- Frobenius norm penalty
	- Lipschitz control
- Learn residual dynamics only (UDE)
- Try an IMEX or semi-implicit split 
	- known physics: explicit
	- learned correction: implicit (or damped)
	- ChatGPT says this is very effective
- Don't trust Tsit45 alone - it can hide stiffness

Diffrax does not have a built in way to access the number of function evaluations. What we have is num_steps, num_accepted_steps, and num_rejected_steps. When we are using an adaptive solver like Tsit5, the solver proposes a step size dt, estimates local truncation error, compares error to tolerance, and if error <= tol we accept the step, otherwise reject step and retry with a smaller dt. After a step is accepted, the solver advanced time successfully. We see many rejected steps if dynamics are stiff. 

Why do we take so few steps? Adaptive solvers take steps based on how long the integration interval is ($t_1 - t_0$), how smooth the vector field is, and how loose/tight tolerances are. If $t_1 - t_0$ is small (which it is when we segment our trajectory) and the learned dynamics are smooth, Tsit5 + PID will take large steps and finish in 1-3 accepted steps. Low step count means the solver thinks the ODE is easy over a short interval, and the learned vector field is smooth. Low step counts are bad if you are expecting high-frequency physics or if the adaptive solver is masking model error - training looks good, rollouts look bad, solver steps are very large. This is a known failure mode for neural ODEs - adaptive solvers can hide sharp errors by shrinking or growing dt. 

Things we can check:
- tighten tolerances - if steps jump from ~2 to ~20-50, then the solver is being lenient and is just integrating coarsely. If it stays low, the dynamics are very smooth and maybe too smooth to capture physics
- Fixed step RK4 ablation
```
solver = dfx.RK4()
dt = (t1 - t0) / 10

If performance degrades, adaptive solver was doing work and low step count wasn't hiding issues. If performance is similar, learned dynamics are simple and bottleneck is model expressivity/inductive bias, not solver. ## Loss functions
1. Velocity-acceleration consistency loss (assumption free)
	$\mathcal{L}_{\text{cons}} = \left\| \frac{d\hat{x}}{dt} - f_\theta(\hat{x}) \right\|^2$ 
	This means that your predicted trajectory should obey your own learned vector field. Useful for long-horizon stability and prevents solver-cheating behavior
2.  One-step / multi-step rollout loss (agnostic but powerful)
	Train with a mix of short, medium and long rolluts. Loss:
	$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{k \in \{1,5,20\}} \|x(t+k) - \hat{x}(t+k)\|^2$ 
	This makes no assumptions about physics, just says that good dynamics should compose. Usually helps improve generalization more than adding physics constraints. 
3. Phase-space geometry loss (still physics-agnostic)
	Instead of comparing states directly, compare local geometry including distances between nearby points, angles between velocity vectors, curvature of trajectories. Example:
	$\mathcal{L}_{\text{geom}} = \left\| \|x_i - x_j\| - \|\hat{x}_i - \hat{x}_j\| \right\|$ 
	This enforces shape and flow geometry without enforcing what that geometry should be. 
4. Distributional trajectory loss (agnostic, but expensive)
	Treat trajectories as distributions in phase space and compare via sliced Wassertein distance, MMD, energy distance (statistical, not physical). This is assumption free but very heavy and more useful for generative models than prediction. Overkill for now.

So it’s more accurate to say “input dimension of the vector field network” than “input layer.”

## Universal Differential Equations
Neural ODE vs UDE:
A neural ODE learns the entire right-hand side of an ODE using a neural network:
$\dot{x}(t)=f_\theta(x(t),t)$ 
e.g. $f_\theta$ is purely learned. No explicit physics, constraints, or structure are included unless you add it manually; the model discovers everything from data. 
A Universal Differential Equation (UDE) keeps known physics and inserts a neural network only where the model is incomplete or uncertain:
$\dot{x}(t)=f_{\textrm{known}}(x,t)+f_\theta(x,t)$ 
e.g. the neural network acts as a closure term, correction , or unknown force. This is much more data-efficient.
