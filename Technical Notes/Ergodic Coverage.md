In high–dimensional dynamical systems it is usually impractical to measure coverage with a uniform grid because the number of cells grows exponentially with dimension. For the CR3BP energy manifold, the state dimension is effectively five, so even modest discretizations become computationally intractable. Because of this, many machine-learning and control papers use **state-space occupancy** or **ergodic coverage** metrics instead. These approaches measure how well a trajectory distribution covers a target region of state space without explicitly discretizing it.

  

The central idea is to treat trajectories as samples from a probability distribution over state space. Suppose the state is x \in \mathbb{R}^n and we observe a trajectory x(t) over time t\in[0,T]. The empirical state distribution generated by the trajectory can be written as

  

$$

c(x) = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T \delta(x - x(t)), dt

$$

  

where \delta is the Dirac delta. This object is called the **occupancy measure** or **time-averaged state distribution**. It describes how often the trajectory visits different regions of the state space.

  

In many coverage problems we also define a **target distribution** \phi(x). This distribution represents how we would ideally like the system to explore the state space. For example:

- uniform over the admissible CR3BP Hill region
    
- concentrated near invariant manifolds
    
- matching the distribution of a training dataset
    

  

Coverage is then defined by comparing the empirical distribution c(x) to the target distribution \phi(x).

  

The most common way to measure the difference between these distributions is the **ergodic metric**, introduced in coverage control literature. Instead of comparing the functions directly in state space, the comparison is done in a Fourier basis. Let F_k(x) be orthonormal basis functions (typically cosine functions on a bounded domain). The coefficients of the trajectory distribution are

  

$$

c_k = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T F_k(x(t)), dt

$$

  

and the coefficients of the target distribution are

  

$$

\phi_k = \int_{\mathcal{D}} \phi(x) F_k(x), dx

$$

  

where \mathcal{D} is the bounded domain of interest.

  

The **ergodic coverage metric** is then

  

$$

E = \sum_k \Lambda_k (c_k - \phi_k)^2

$$

  

where the weights

  

$$

\Lambda_k = \frac{1}{(1 + |k|^2)^s}

$$

  

downweight high-frequency modes so that large-scale spatial mismatches dominate the metric.

  

Interpretation is straightforward. If the trajectory spends time in state space proportional to the desired distribution \phi(x), then the coefficients c_k will match \phi_k and the metric E will be small. If the trajectory explores only a small portion of the state space, many coefficients will differ and the metric will be large.

  

This method avoids explicit discretization of the state space. Instead of dividing the space into bins, it compares the **spectral content** of the trajectory distribution and the target distribution. The computational cost scales with the number of Fourier modes rather than the dimension of the state space.

  

For evaluating learned dynamical models, this approach is particularly useful. Suppose you have

- a dataset of CR3BP trajectories used for training
    
- rollouts from a learned neural ODE model
    

  

You can compute occupancy coefficients for both trajectory sets

  

$$

c_k^{\text{data}}, \qquad c_k^{\text{model}}

$$

  

and compare them using the same ergodic metric

  

$$

E = \sum_k \Lambda_k (c_k^{\text{model}} - c_k^{\text{data}})^2.

$$

  

If the learned dynamics reproduce the same phase-space exploration as the true system, the occupancy statistics will match and the metric will be small.

  

This idea is widely used in robotics and dynamical systems learning. A foundational reference is

  

Mathew & Mezić (2011), _Metrics for ergodicity and design of ergodic dynamics for multi-agent systems_, Physica D.

  

and it appears in many coverage-control and exploration algorithms, including work by

  

Miller & Murphey (2013), _Trajectory optimization for continuous ergodic exploration_.

  

For evaluating learned ODE models, the advantage is that this metric measures **global phase-space exploration** without needing a high-dimensional grid. It is therefore well suited for systems like the CR3BP where the true state space is five-dimensional after applying the Jacobi constraint.
